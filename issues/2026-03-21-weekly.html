Lead Story

Iran, Day 21: The War Nobody Knows How to End

Twenty-one days ago, the United States and Israel launched Operation Epic Fury, killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and triggering what has become the defining crisis of 2026. Three weeks in, every metric suggests escalation rather than resolution. Oil prices have surged more than 40% since the war began. The Strait of Hormuz — through which nearly a fifth of the world's oil flows — is effectively blocked. And despite early confidence in Washington that the operation would be swift, the war has metastasised in ways that defy the original four-week timetable.

The past week alone tells the story. On 18 March, Israel struck Iran's South Pars gas field — one of the largest in the world. Iran retaliated against Qatar's LNG facility. Al Udeid Air Base in Qatar, a critical hub for US operations, was hit by an Iranian ballistic missile. The Iranian frigate IRIS Dena was sunk by a US submarine. Iran's new Supreme Leader — Mojtaba Khamenei, son of Ali — has shown no interest in capitulation. Lebanese Hezbollah was drawn in on 2 March, and Israel launched a ground invasion of southern Lebanon on 17 March, displacing 800,000 people.

The human cost continues to mount: more than 1,400 Iranian civilian deaths, 204 of them children, and over 18,000 injuries. The UAE, Kuwait, and Saudi Arabia have all been struck by Iranian missiles, globalising the conflict well beyond its intended scope. NATO allies have refused Trump's call for help securing the Strait, prompting public rebukes. And on Day 22, Trump began talking about "winding down" — a phrase that sounded less like strategy and more like recognition that there is no clean exit.

Russia is reportedly providing intelligence to Iran — a development that underscores how the conflict has become a proxy theatre for broader geopolitical competition. Trump has mulled lifting some Iranian oil sanctions as a concession, an extraordinary reversal for an administration that launched the war partly to prevent Iran's nuclear programme from advancing. The UK Foreign Secretary has warned Iran against targeting British bases, a statement that reveals just how wide the potential escalation corridor has become. None of these moves suggest a power that is winning on its own terms.

The economic reverberations extend well beyond the Middle East. European natural gas prices have spiked as LNG supply disruptions ripple through global markets. Shipping insurance premiums in the Gulf have tripled. Airlines are rerouting around Iranian airspace at significant cost. And in the background, a quieter disruption: the fertiliser supply chains that depend on Gulf petrochemicals are under strain, with agricultural economists warning of downstream food price impacts by summer. This war was supposed to be surgical. It is becoming systemic.

The Angle

The real story here isn't the military campaign — it's the absence of an endgame. Iran has proven regime resilience: kill the Supreme Leader and his son steps in. Close the Strait and the economic disruption globalises. Trump's four-week timetable has passed with no visible pathway to resolution. The Hormuz closure alone has already changed global energy markets in ways that won't reverse when the shooting stops. This war isn't entering a new phase. It never left the first one.

Did You Know?

The Strait of Hormuz is only 21 miles wide at its narrowest point — roughly the width of the English Channel at Dover. Nearly 20% of the world's oil passes through it daily. When it closes, there is no alternative route of comparable capacity anywhere on Earth.

Blue Prince Is Out on Switch 2 — And It's Exactly the Kind of Game the Platform Needs

Blue Prince is here. The genre-defying mystery game — part strategy, part puzzle, part architectural riddle — launched on Switch 2 this week, and if you've had it on your radar, it's time. You play as an heir exploring a mansion where the rooms rearrange themselves every run. Each playthrough reveals new corridors, new rules, new reasons to doubt what you thought you understood about the house and the family that built it.

This isn't a port of an action game struggling to fit on portable hardware. It's exactly the kind of smart, patient indie that feels purpose-built for playing in handheld mode with a coffee on a Sunday morning. The Switch 2 runs it beautifully, and the game's deliberate pacing — think lateral thinking over twitch reflexes — rewards the kind of quiet attention that a tablet in your hands encourages. If you've been looking for the game that justifies the Switch 2 as a platform for thoughtful play, this is a very strong candidate.

The Angle

The real story here isn't just a good indie launch — it's what Blue Prince represents for the Switch 2 ecosystem. A solo developer (Tonda Ros made the entire game alone) creating something that feels native to the hardware's strengths. Not a scaled-down AAA port. Not a phone game with buttons. A genuine, genre-defining experience that makes the case: this is what handheld gaming should be.

Did You Know?

Blue Prince was developed entirely by solo developer Tonda Ros. Every room, every puzzle, every shifting corridor in the game — all made by one person.

The sets themselves look excellent on their own merits, but the app layer adds replayability that static display sets lack.

The broader March wave is strong across other themes too: LEGO Legend of Zelda 77093 Ocarina of Time — The Final Battle, LEGO Icons 11373 Sauron's Helmet and 11389 Project Hail Mary (yes, the Andy Weir novel), and LEGO Ideas sets including 21366 Floating Sea Otter and 21376 Orange Cat. Lead — Now Streaming

Peaky Blinders: The Immortal Man — Ceremony, Not Sequel

The long-awaited film continuation arrived on Netflix this week, and the first thing to understand is what it isn't: it isn't the show at its sharpest. Set in 1940, with a Second World War backdrop, The Immortal Man finds Tommy Shelby (Cillian Murphy) in self-imposed exile, haunted, writing a memoir that functions as both confession and justification. Barry Keoghan plays Duke, his estranged son, now heading the next generation of Blinders. A Nazi counterfeit currency plot draws Tommy back into the world he tried to leave behind.

Critical reception is split. Parade gave it 9/10, calling it "an emotionally charged conclusion" with Murphy delivering a career-defining performance. The New York Times was cooler: "flat caps and inflated myths," criticising the film's tendency to mythologise its protagonist and singling out Tim Roth's Nazi villain as ill-suited. Esquire landed somewhere between — "deeply flawed but with redeeming qualities," praising a mud fight scene and Keoghan's magnetic screen presence. And Film Ireland offered the most interesting read: the film is "ruminative, more concerned with repercussions of violence than fan service" — a meditation on mental health and trauma wrapped in the gangster aesthetic.

The Signal's take: The Immortal Man is less a film than a ceremony — it exists to close the coffin on Tommy Shelby rather than give him one last great story. Murphy does everything asked of him, Keoghan earns every second of screen time, and the WW2 setting gives the production design somewhere new to breathe. But the Nazi plot feels thin against the weight of what came before, and Tim Roth is miscast in a role that needed menace it never quite finds. Worth watching. Essential for fans. But temper expectations if you're hoping for the show at its best.

The Angle

The real story here isn't whether the film is good — it's whether the TV-to-film transition can ever truly satisfy. Peaky Blinders earned six seasons of character depth, moral complexity, and atmospheric storytelling. Compressing a farewell into two hours forces the kind of mythologising that the best episodes avoided. The Immortal Man is a memorial service disguised as a heist film, and if you accept it on those terms, it works. If you want the Garrison pub, the razor blades, and the unpredictability — that was always going to live in the series.

Five Books for Malazan Survivors and Fantasy Wanderers

Children of Strife

Adrian Tchaikovsky — Out now (March 2026)

Tchaikovsky continues to be one of the most reliable names in speculative fiction. If you've read Children of Time — and if you haven't, stop what you're doing — this latest instalment in his universe expands the scope further. His ability to make non-human perspectives genuinely alien, yet emotionally resonant, remains unmatched.

Seasons of Glass and Iron: Stories

Amal El-Mohtar — Tordotcom, March 24

The first collection from the co-author of This Is How You Lose the Time War. El-Mohtar writes with the precision of poetry and the momentum of prose, and this collection gathers her short fiction into one place for the first time. If Time War caught something in your chest, this is where you go next.

Between Two Fires

Christopher Buehlman — Tor rerelease, March 3

Buehlman wrote The Blacktongue Thief — and if you enjoyed that, this dark historical fantasy is where he started. Set during the Black Death, following a disgraced knight and a girl who sees angels across a France that is literally coming apart. Grotesque, beautiful, and genuinely terrifying. One of the best dark fantasies of the last decade, now reissued.

No Man's Land

Richard K. Morgan — March 24

Morgan, the mind behind Altered Carbon, goes to the First World War — and adds ancient fae called the Huldu who've decided humanity's time should end. Dark, literary, and uncompromising. Morgan's prose has always had weight to it; applied to the trenches, it's devastating.

The Prince of Nothing (The Second Apocalypse)

R. Scott Bakker — Backlist recommendation

If you're currently deep in Malazan and want to know what comes after — what's the next series that matches that scope, that moral darkness, that sense of civilisations grinding against each other — the answer is Bakker. The Darkness That Comes Before is the entry point. It borrows from the Crusades, adds a protagonist who might be a messiah or a sociopath (the series never quite decides, and that's the point), and builds a world with the philosophical density of Malazan but an entirely different texture. It is not easy. It is extraordinary.

Synthwave Dispatches

The synthwave and retrowave scene continues to pulse quietly beneath mainstream consciousness. Names like Perturbator, Carpenter Brut, and Dance With the Dead sit at this intersection, and they're worth exploring if the lighter end of synthwave feels too smooth for your taste.

The Kettlebell Mile: A Deceptively Simple Test That Doesn't Lie

StrongFirst published a piece that distills two decades of research into one beautifully simple concept: carry a kettlebell for one mile. Time yourself. That's it. The Kettlebell Mile — a suitcase carry, switching hands as needed — tests your grip, your posture, your aerobic capacity, and your mental resolve simultaneously. It is the Dan John philosophy made physical: if you can do this well, you're fit. If you can't, you know exactly what needs work.

The research behind it is compelling. Strength training alone improves loaded-carry performance. Running alone improves endurance. But combining the two — strength plus running — produces results greater than either alone. The Kettlebell Mile sits at the intersection: it's not a strength test, it's not a cardio test, it's a hybrid test. And for anyone following a general-athleticism approach, that's exactly the benchmark that matters.

Pace Time Assessment
Fast walk ~15 min/mile Starting point — build from here
Jogging pace 11–13 min Solid general fitness
Strong pace 9–11 min Very good hybrid capacity
Elite < 9 min Exceptional — few reach this

The progression model is simple: increase distance or load first, then speed. Don't chase the clock until the distance and weight feel manageable. A 24kg kettlebell for one mile at a jog is a very different proposition than the same weight at a walk — respect the ramp.

What makes this test compelling from a Dan John perspective is its honesty. You can't fake it. A barbell back squat lets you compensate with technique adjustments. A treadmill run lets you hold the rails. But a suitcase carry for a measured distance with a clock running reveals exactly where you are: your grip fails or it doesn't, your posture degrades or it holds, your lungs keep up or they don't. There's no hiding. And the beauty is in the simplicity — you need a kettlebell, a route, and a stopwatch. No gym required. The road from your front door to the end of the village and back is your testing ground.

StrongFirst also has an SFG Kettlebell Certification coming to McLean, Virginia in May 2026 — not local, but notable for serious practitioners who want to formalise their practice.

Thomas Cranmer: The Hand That Burned First

Thomas Cranmer was the first Protestant Archbishop of Canterbury, the architect of the Church of England's theological foundation, and the man who gave the English language its most enduring sacred prose through the Book of Common Prayer of 1549. He had reshaped a nation's faith. And on this day in 1556, he was burned at the stake in Oxford on the orders of the Catholic Queen Mary I.

The tragedy is in the details. Facing execution, Cranmer had broken. He signed six separate recantations of his Protestant beliefs — each one a capitulation, each one written in the hope that the queen would spare him. She would not. On the morning of 21 March, brought to St Mary's Church to publicly read his final recantation before the crowd, Cranmer instead withdrew every word he had written. He declared his recantations false, born of fear and weakness, and said his right hand — the hand that had signed them — "hath offended" and would be punished first.

When the flames were lit at the stake, Cranmer thrust his right hand into the fire and held it there, burning, refusing to withdraw it. Eyewitness accounts record that he did not pull it back. The hand that had written the words he could not live with was the first thing he chose to destroy. He was seventy years old. His prayer book is still read in churches today — nearly five hundred years later, his prose shapes Anglican worship. The hand burned. The words survived.

Did You Know?

Thomas Cranmer's Book of Common Prayer (1549) still forms the basis of Anglican worship today. His prose is directly quoted in Church of England services nearly 500 years later — including the marriage vows that begin "Dearly beloved, we are gathered together here in the sight of God."

Also This Week in History

  • 22 March 1312
    Pope Clement V dissolves the Knights Templar via the papal bull Vox in excelso, ending two hundred years of the most powerful military-religious order in Christendom. The Templars had survived Crusades, sieges, and the fall of the Holy Land — but they could not survive a French king who wanted their money and a pope who lacked the spine to refuse him.
  • 23 March 1801
    Tsar Paul I of Russia is murdered by his own guards in the Mikhailovsky Castle — beaten, strangled with a sash, stamped on. His own son, Alexander, knew about the plot and almost certainly approved it. The new Tsar would go on to defeat Napoleon. His father's body was still warm.
  • 25 March 44 BC
    Julius Caesar's assassins flee Rome after the public turns against them following Mark Antony's funeral oration. The Ides of March (15 March) had already passed — but it was in this week that the conspiracy truly unravelled, and Brutus discovered that killing a tyrant and replacing a tyrant are very different skills.
