Iran War: Week Four, No Exit, No Strategy, No Ceiling on Oil

Marines Deployed

The US–Israel military campaign against Iran entered its fourth week with no ceasefire in sight. President Trump called NATO allies "cowards" on 20 March for refusing to join the effort to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, while simultaneously suggesting he might "wind down" US operations after claiming victory. The contradiction is the strategy — or rather, the absence of one.

Iran, far from finished, has struck oil infrastructure across the region, threatened to target American and Israeli officials at tourist sites worldwide, and tightened its chokehold on the Strait of Hormuz — through which one-fifth of the world's oil flows. A joint US–Israel airstrike hit Iran's Natanz nuclear enrichment site on 21 March; local media said no radioactive release occurred. The IRGC published video appearing to show a US F-35 struck over Iranian airspace; the Pentagon confirmed only an "emergency landing."

The second-order effects are already cascading. Iran's attacks halted helium exports from Qatar — previously more than one-third of global supply. Helium isn't just for party balloons: it's essential for MRI machines, semiconductor fabrication, and space launches. Five European nations and Japan signalled they would "contribute" to Hormuz security, though what that means in practice remains deliberately vague. Oil analysts warn prices could hit $150–200 per barrel if the conflict continues.

Al Jazeera's analysis is the most clear-eyed: the war is unlikely to produce a clear winner. The most realistic near-term goal is a ceasefire beginning with a moratorium on strikes against energy infrastructure. Trump told reporters: "You don't initiate a ceasefire when you are in the process of thoroughly defeating the opposing side." The problem, of course, is that nobody is being thoroughly defeated.

The Angle

The real story isn't the airstrikes — it's the price of heating oil in Ballymena. The Iran war is no longer a geopolitical abstraction. It is reshaping the cost of living for half a million Northern Irish households, the future of NATO solidarity, and the global energy supply chain. The helium shortage alone could disrupt hospital imaging across Europe within months.

Orbán Holds Ukraine's €90 Billion Ransom

The EU summit in Brussels ended in failure. Twenty-five of 27 member states backed a €90 billion loan for Ukraine; unanimity is required. Hungary's Viktor Orbán blocked it single-handedly, tying the release of funds to restoration of Russian oil deliveries through the Druzhba pipeline. Slovakia also refused to sign the conclusions. German Chancellor Friedrich Merz called it "an act of serious disloyalty." EU Commission President Von der Leyen promised to "deliver one way or another." The next summit is late April. Ukraine needs the money by early May to remain solvent.

Orbán faces elections on 12 April and appears to be using the standoff as an electoral tool. The twin crises of Iran and Ukraine are now entangled at the EU level — the summit conclusions also called for a "moratorium on strikes against energy and water facilities" in the Middle East.

NI Heating Oil: Iran's War Made Personal

Northern Ireland's dependence on home heating oil — an estimated 68% of households, approximately 480,600 homes — has made the Iran war's energy shock acutely painful here. Oil prices rose over 80% in less than a week in early March. The UK Government allocated £17.2 million in emergency support, which equates to roughly £35 per affected household. Finance Minister John O'Dowd called it "far short of what is required."

The Utility Regulator told the NI Assembly that gas and electricity prices will rise further "if the Iran conflict lasts another month or two." Alliance MLA David Honeyford told the Assembly on 16 March that "families in Lagan Valley have just seen their heating bills double in a matter of weeks" while Stormont produced "political pantomime" instead of a plan. A motion on 24 March will debate "Renewables to Enhance Northern Ireland's Energy Security."

Did You Know?

Northern Ireland has the highest rate of household reliance on heating oil in the entire United Kingdom. Most of Great Britain is connected to the natural gas grid; NI's rural housing stock means oil tanks in the garden remain the norm for the majority of homes.

SteamOS 3.8.0: The Steam Machine Is Actually Happening

Valve released SteamOS 3.8.0 Preview on 19–20 March, and the headline is "initial support for upcoming Steam Machine hardware" — the first concrete sign of the console's imminent launch. This is the most significant Steam Deck update in over a year.

Key features: KDE Plasma updated to 6.4.3 with Wayland as default, Linux kernel bumped to 6.16, HDR display support, VRR display support, per-display scale factor, and improved Discord/OBS screencasting in Game Mode (long-awaited fix). Valve previously reaffirmed: "Nothing has actually changed on our end" regarding a 2026 Steam Machine launch. The runway is built.

Putting Bluetooth chips in minifigures is either a stroke of genius or the beginning of a subscription trap. Either way, it's the most interesting LEGO story in decades.

The Bracket That Football Built

The Champions League Round of 16 concluded over two extraordinary nights, and the quarter-final draw that emerged may be the strongest since the peak Pep-Klopp era. PSG vs Liverpool is a rematch with history. Real Madrid vs Bayern is a fixture that needs no introduction. Barcelona vs Atlético is a derby elevated to the European stage. Sporting vs Arsenal is the underdog story the neutral hopes survives longest.

The numbers tell the story of the round. Chelsea were humiliated 8–2 on aggregate by PSG — the worst European aggregate in their history. Real Madrid demolished Man City 5–1, knocking them out for the third consecutive year. Bernardo Silva, ironically linked with Juventus as a free agent, received a straight red for handball on the line — his first red in 104 CL appearances. Vinicius Júnior converted the penalty. Barcelona put seven past Newcastle in one of the most brutal Camp Nou nights in memory. Lewandowski and Raphinha both scored braces.

Arsenal's passage was calmer but no less convincing: Eberechi Eze scored his first-ever Champions League goal, a top-corner rocket, and Declan Rice added the second in a 2–0 win over Leverkusen (3–1 on aggregate). Sporting's remontada against Bodø/Glimt — down 3–0 from the first leg, winning 5–0 on the night — was the first three-goal comeback since Liverpool's iconic 4–0 vs Barcelona in 2019.

Five English clubs entered the last 16. Two survive. Chelsea gutted. City humiliated. Newcastle dismantled. The island's hegemony in European football is wobbling — and the QF field is arguably better for it.

The Old Lady Stirs — But the David Problem Won't Go Away

Juventus beat Udinese 1–0 (Boga, 38', assisted by Yildiz) for their second successive victory, pushing them to fifth with 53 points — one behind Como in the final Champions League spot. Jérémie Boga has been the unexpected hero of Juve's recent run, displacing Jonathan David from the starting XI.

Since Spalletti took over from Tudor on 30 October, only Inter (50 pts) and Milan (42 pts) have collected more points in Serie A than Juventus (38 pts). The improvement is real but the ceiling is CL qualification, not the Scudetto. Spalletti is close to signing a new deal through 2027 (option for 2028) at €6m/year, making him Serie A's second-highest-paid coach behind Conte. A decisive meeting with DoF Chiellini was expected this week.

Jonathan David's experiment looks over. Five goals in 28 Serie A appearances. Juve are exploring a summer sale at roughly €30m — still profit on a free transfer from Lille, but a sign of how far his stock has fallen. Boga's emergence and Vlahovic's imminent return from injury make David the third striker at a club that can carry two.

The Take

Juve's absence from this year's UCL knockouts — eliminated 7–5 by Galatasaray in the playoffs — is the quiet backdrop to all their summer planning. Without CL football next season, the Bernardo Silva deal gets harder, the Greenwood deal disappears entirely, and Tonali is beyond them. Everything hinges on finishing in the top four. One point separates them from Como. This is a club season defined by arithmetic.

Project Hail Mary Opens to Rapturous Reviews

The space film you've been waiting for opened on 20 March to the kind of reviews that make you check if you're misreading the numbers. Directed by Phil Lord and Christopher Miller (The LEGO Movie, Into the Spider-Verse) — their first co-directorial effort since 22 Jump Street in 2014 — and starring Ryan Gosling as molecular biologist Ryland Grace, waking alone in deep space to save humanity from an alien energy parasite consuming stars.

The film earned $12 million in preview screenings alone. Tom's Guide called it "easily the best new release of 2026 so far." SlashFilm described it as "a dazzling, heartfelt intergalactic caper." The alien co-star "Rocky" was created largely through puppetry — a technique Lord and Miller refined during their time on Solo before being fired by Lucasfilm. The Solo dismissal, it turns out, forged the creature-shop relationships that made Project Hail Mary possible. Shot in two aspect ratios by cinematographer Greig Fraser. Andy Weir was a producer and "involved at every step."

The timing is perfect: Children of Strife (Adrian Tchaikovsky) landed the same week. Both are about alien communication, species-level stakes, and the possibility that intelligence — however alien — can be a force for good. This is optimistic sci-fi's banner moment.

Tchaikovsky's Spider Epic Gets Its Best Chapter Yet

Children of Strife (March 17 US / March 26 UK) is the fourth book in Adrian Tchaikovsky's Children of Time series — approximately 700 pages, published by Tor. Three timelines spanning thousands of years: a doomed terraforming project, ark ships fleeing Earth, and the familiar crew of Avrana Kern, Cato the Mantis Shrimp, the Portid spiders, and human/AI hybrids confronting a mystery aboard the Dissenter.

The Quill to Live: "I have no notes on any areas of improvement. This is an easy early contender for a top book of 2026." Fantasy-Hive: "an extraordinary next volume featuring epic adventure, first contact, and the nature of intelligence." Can be read as a standalone (includes a "What Has Gone Before" summary), but the emotional payoff is richer with the first three under your belt.

The timing with Project Hail Mary in cinemas is perfect — both are about alien communication, survival, and the stubborn possibility that contact with the radically other might be the best thing that ever happened to us.

The Session

Why the Kettlebell Is the Smartest Cross-Training Tool a Runner Can Own

Three data points converged this week that make the case for adding kettlebell work to a running programme — not as a replacement, not as a trendy add-on, but as the thing that makes your running sustainable.

The practitioner's case: Runner's World published Mallory Creveling's breakdown of kettlebell training for runners. Creveling (ACE-certified PT, RRCA run coach, kettlebell certified since 2019) argues that "kettlebell training adds metabolic conditioning to weight training, builds strength, precision, power, and coordination — complementing running's repetitive linear motion." Key exercises: the swing (posterior chain, hip hinge power), the snatch (full-body explosiveness — 80 reps in 5 minutes with 12kg is the certification test), the Turkish get-up (stabiliser strength, shoulder health), and complexes like single-arm swing → clean → diagonal lunge.

The science: An American Physiological Society study (published 2024, "Strength after Sixty" programme) found that 12 months of twice-weekly kettlebell training in adults aged 60–80 increased muscle mass, grip strength, upper-leg strength, and reduced whole-body inflammation markers. A separate Science Direct RCT showed kettlebell exercises reduce relative cardiopulmonary effort for daily activities in patients with pulmonary arterial hypertension.

The coaching argument: Elite running coach Jay Johnson advocates for a post-run strength block as the fourth element of every training session — "extend the aerobic stimulus" by keeping heart rate elevated through strength work after easy runs. The metabolic adaptation, he notes, is faster than structural adaptation — good news for older or injury-prone runners.

The frame: the kettlebell swing is not an alternative to running. It's what makes your running sustainable.

Did You Know?

The kettlebell snatch certification test — sometimes called "the Secret Service Snatch Test" — requires 100 snatches in 5 minutes with a 24kg bell for men. The standard entry-level test is 80 reps in 5 minutes with 12kg. It is, by most accounts, one of the most unpleasant five minutes in fitness.

This Week in History

The Birmingham Six: 35 Years Since Walking Free

On 14 March 1991 — 35 years ago this week — six Irishmen walked free from the Court of Appeal in London after serving 16 years in prison for a crime they did not commit. Hugh Callaghan, Patrick Hill, Gerard Hunter, Richard McIlkenny, William Power, and John Walker had been convicted in 1975 for the bombing of two Birmingham pubs on 21 November 1974 that killed 21 people.

The police had fabricated evidence. The forensic science used to convict them was subsequently discredited. Their convictions were quashed as "unsafe and unsatisfactory." They were awarded financial compensation ranging from £840,000 to £1.2 million — roughly £50,000 for each year of a stolen life.

Their exoneration directly contributed to the establishment of the Criminal Cases Review Commission in 1997. Paul Hill of the Guildford Four — himself a victim of wrongful conviction — was present in the crowd outside the court. Richard McIlkenny died in 2006. The others have lived with the legacy ever since. The British state has still not issued a formal apology.

The Birmingham Six walked free into a world without social media, without smartphones. Their names were already known to every household in Ireland and Britain through years of campaigning. What would it look like today?
