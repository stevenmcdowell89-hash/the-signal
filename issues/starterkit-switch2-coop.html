There's a particular moment — two players sharing a couch, one controller each, something genuinely difficult happening on screen — that no online session has ever replicated. The Switch 2 era is producing more of those moments than any platform in recent memory. GameShare, backward compatibility with a decade of great Switch co-op games, and a new generation of titles designed for genuine equal-role play have quietly made this the best time to be playing games side by side. Co-Op
Why This Matters
What if the best gaming experience of the year cost one copy, two controllers, and a couch?
Nintendo's own vision of what Switch co-op looks like. The Switch 2 makes it better.

For most of gaming history, co-op has been a polite fiction. One player drives; the other watches, occasionally contributes, and waits to be respawned. The original New Super Mario Bros. dynamic — Player 1 completes the level, Player 2 bounces in a bubble — is so deeply embedded in game design that it became the default template for a generation of co-op games.

The Switch 2 era breaks from it. Not because of hardware specs, but because of a structural shift in what "co-op" means to developers. The best games shipping right now are built on the premise that both players are protagonists. In Hyrule Warriors: Age of Imprisonment, both players choose from the same roster with no restriction. In Sunderfolk, either player can act at any time. In Sea of Stars, the Timed Hits system means Player 2 is actively contributing to every single attack — not waiting for their turn to arrive.

This is equal-role design, and it's the single most important shift in co-op gaming in a decade.

The Signal Take
  • GameShare doesn't just save money. It removes the activation barrier — the moment where someone has to buy in before they know if they'll like a game.
  • One purchase, two Switch 2s, instant experiment. That's a different product than anything the PS5 or Xbox offers right now.
  • Switch 2 backward-compatible library — 4,000+ games
  • Co-op games in Switch library — 800+
  • GameShare supported titles at launch — 40+

The Switch 2 plays the entire Switch 1 library, which means the co-op catalogue available on day one of owning the console is enormous. Titles from 2017 — the original Switch launch year — are fully accessible. Games like Risk of Rain Returns, Trine: Ultimate Collection, and Sea of Stars that came out on Switch 1 work natively, often with performance improvements on the new hardware.

This Starter Kit reflects that reality. Five of the seven essential picks are Switch 1 titles. The best co-op game on the platform right now — TMNT: Splintered Fate — isn't even marketed as a Switch 2 title. The platform's back catalogue is where most of the value is. Any buying guide that limits itself to Switch 2 exclusives is missing the point.

Did You Know?

GameShare on Switch 2 works by letting a second nearby console download and temporarily run a full copy of a digital game — not a streamed screen. The original purchaser maintains access at all times. The experience is identical on both consoles, with no latency gap.

01. TMNT: Splintered Fate ★ Top Pick

Roguelite Brawler · Couch Co-Op · Up to 4 Players · Switch (BC) · ~£20 · PEGI 7
"If you know Hades, you already understand half the game. The other half is that you're a Turtle." — widely cited in Switch co-op recommendation threads

From the same structural DNA as Hades — roguelite runs, permanent upgrades between deaths, multiple characters with distinct builds — but wearing one of the most iconic IPs in animation. Each Turtle plays differently: Donnie heals the party on room entry, Raph crits more often, Leo balances offense and defense, Mikey brings chaos. The run-based structure keeps sessions to 30-60 minutes, which is exactly right for shared couch play.

What transforms this from good to essential is how co-op changes the ability calculus. Coordinating Turtle powers across a boss fight — Donnie's team heals keeping everyone alive while Raph stacks crits — is the kind of emergent teamwork that makes couch gaming worth having. The Dimension Shellshock DLC adds a Survival Mode for players who exhaust the main campaign.

02. Risk of Rain Returns The Infinite Ceiling

Roguelite Platformer · Shared Screen · Switch (BC) · ~£15 · PEGI 7
  • 15 unlockable Survivors, each with completely distinct playstyles
  • Difficulty scales with elapsed time — longer runs get exponentially harder
  • Item stacking creates absurd power fantasy: both players may be firing random missiles while quintuple-jumping
  • PEGI 7 despite genuine difficulty — no blood, no horror

    • The "roguelite with an infinite ceiling" description isn't hyperbole: if the core loop clicks, Risk of Rain Returns has hundreds of hours of replayability built in. Each Survivor unlocks via in-run challenges, so the game is constantly revealing something new. The shared-screen co-op means both players are always in the same frame, which forces organic communication — "I'm going left, cover the portal" — that defines good couch play.

    The signature tension mechanic — difficulty that increases in real-time rather than in waves — means sessions have natural drama. A 45-minute run that's going well suddenly becomes precarious. A great loop.

    The question to ask: Do you find the "one more run" roguelite loop addictive? If yes, this has more hours in it than anything else on this list.

    03. Sunderfolk The Wildcard

    Tactical RPG · Digital Board Game · 1–4 Players · Switch (BC) · ~£25 · E10+ · 91st Percentile OpenCritic
    6
    Character classes — crow arcanist, polar bear berserker, salamander pyromancer, and more

    Nothing else on Switch plays like this. Each player controls their character via their own smartphone — scan a QR code and you're in. The phone becomes your private hand of cards: you see your options, opponents don't. This is Gloomhaven for the living room, without 400 cardboard tokens and a three-hour setup time.

    Turn order is genuinely flexible — any player can act at any time within the phase — which creates a tactical texture where timing matters as much as positioning. The 20-30 hour campaign rewards investment. Its developers previously worked at Blizzard; the polish is visible in everything from the tutorial to the endgame scaling.

    05. Sea of Stars The Surprise Pick

    Turn-Based RPG · Shared Screen · Up to 3 Players · Switch (BC) · ~£30 · PEGI 7 · Metacritic 91
    The problem with RPG co-op: Most attempts at it are boring. One player controls, the other watches cutscenes and waits for their turn. Sea of Stars solved this in a way no other RPG has.

    A Metacritic 91 RPG that added full co-op in a free post-launch update called Dawn of Equinox. The core innovation is the Timed Hits system applied to multiple players simultaneously: both players press buttons at the precise right moment to enhance attacks or defend, even when it's technically not their turn. Neither player is ever a spectator. The result is co-op that feels designed from the ground up, not tacked on.

    The game itself is 25-35 hours of gorgeous pixel art, a story that earns its emotional moments, and a soundtrack by Yasunori Mitsuda. Drop-in/drop-out means either player can step away without disrupting the other's save — a practical feature that matters for real-life couch sessions.

    06. Trine: Ultimate Collection Puzzle Co-Op That Demands Teamwork

    Puzzle Platformer · Couch Co-Op · 2–4 Players · Switch (BC) · ~£35 · PEGI 7 · 4 Complete Games

    Four complete games of fairy-tale puzzle-platforming, bundled together. The magic of Trine is character role design: Wizard conjures physics objects from thin air, Thief grapples and freezes, Knight bashes and shields. Every puzzle has multiple valid solutions, and the co-op puzzles are deliberately harder than solo — that calibration is intentional and brilliant.

    The art direction is among the most beautiful on any platform: painted fantasy environments, soft lighting, careful compositions. At roughly 24-32 hours across all four games, this is exceptional value. And because each puzzle can be solved multiple ways, replays with different character combinations reveal new paths. Communication is mandatory. That's the whole point.

    07. Diablo III: Eternal Collection The Endless Endgame

    Action RPG · Loot Game · Up to 4 Players · Switch (BC) · ~£25-35 · PEGI 16
  • Seven distinct character classes, each with deep build trees
  • Individual loot drops — both players get gear tailored to their character
  • Console-exclusive dodge-roll mechanic many veterans prefer to the PC version
  • Torment difficulty tiers scale infinitely; endgame never runs out
  • PEGI 16 for stylised blood and demonic imagery — preview solo first

    • The deepest game on this list. If the content rating is acceptable, Diablo III: Eternal Collection is the co-op game that genuinely never runs out. Normal difficulty is a warmup; Torment tiers provide scaling challenge that rewards systematic gear optimisation. The shared-screen co-op means both players are always in the action, and individual loot drops prevent the inventory friction that ruins so many loot games.

    Seasons add new mechanics and progression systems every few months, making this a game that's still being actively played nearly fifteen years after its original release. That's the ceiling here.

    Content note — expand before deciding

    PEGI 16 for stylised blood, demonic imagery, and horror themes. The violence is cartoonish rather than realistic — more "animated skeleton explodes into gold coins" than anything disturbing. That said: a preview solo session before introducing co-op play is the right call. The aesthetic is dark fantasy, not realistic violence.

    Common Mistakes — Buy This, Not That

    Five Things That Cost Players Money

    The Switch co-op shelf is full of games that look like co-op and aren't. These are the most common expensive mistakes, and the alternatives that fix them.

    Mistake 01
    Buying Donkey Kong Bananza for co-op

    Player 2 (Pauline) rides DK's shoulder and fires from a targeting cursor. It's the exact same dynamic as Mario Galaxy's Luma — cosmetically present, mechanically inert. An Inverse review called it "Odyssey's Cappy dynamic revisited." Outstanding solo game; genuinely poor co-op design. Every game in this Starter Kit was selected because neither player is a passenger.

    Mistake 02
    Pikmin 4 instead of Pikmin 3 Deluxe

    Pikmin 4's co-op mode gives Player 2 a pebble-throwing cursor. That's it. Game Rant's explainer describes it as a companion assist mode, not genuine cooperative play. Pikmin 3 Deluxe, by contrast, has genuine split-screen co-op where both players control separate captains with independent Pikmin squads. Both players are required to progress. That's what co-op looks like.

    Mistake 03
    Assuming "multiplayer" means couch co-op

    Splatoon 3's Salmon Run is excellent co-op — but requires two separate Switch consoles. Ravenswatch is online only. Always check the co-op mode explicitly before buying. The Nintendo eShop now lists co-op types on product pages; use it.

    "If the co-op mode involves one player holding a cursor, it's not co-op. It's a demo."

    — The Signal, Issue #7
    Mistake 04
    Overlooking backward-compatible Switch 1 games

    The Switch 2 plays the entire Switch library. Five of the seven picks in this issue are Switch 1 titles. Searching only "Switch 2 games" misses most of the value. Search "Switch" to include everything.

    Mistake 05
    Buying two copies before checking GameShare

    Switch 2's GameShare lets a second console play from one digital purchase for supported titles. Hyrule Warriors: Age of Imprisonment supports it. Check the Pure Nintendo review and the Nintendo site's GameShare list before doubling up.

    One-Week Plan — A Structured Entry

    Your First Seven Sessions

    A practical onramp. The order is deliberate — each new game builds on instincts developed in the previous one. Don't rush it.

    Days 1–2
    TMNT: Splintered Fate

    Complete 2-3 runs. Let everyone choose their Turtle and experiment with how each plays differently. Raph for aggression, Donnie for support — the styles will reveal themselves naturally. Don't read a guide yet. Let the game teach its own vocabulary. Losing the first few runs is the point; the permanent upgrades between runs make each attempt meaningfully better. Goal: Learn each Turtle's role. Win one complete run.

    Day 3
    Risk of Rain Returns — change of pace

    Load Risk of Rain Returns for contrast. Unlock 2-3 Survivors, complete a full run regardless of outcome. The shared screen and real-time difficulty scaling will feel completely different from TMNT's room-clearing pace. That contrast is the lesson — roguelite co-op has multiple distinct flavours, and knowing both expands the vocabulary. Goal: Unlock Huntress and Mercenary. Feel the difference in pacing.

    Days 4–5
    Hyrule Warriors: Age of Imprisonment

    Load Hyrule Warriors — use GameShare if two Switch 2 consoles are available. Push through the tutorial, which runs about 45 minutes before co-op unlocks. Once it opens up, try both hero roles and experiment with Sync Strikes. The scale difference from TMNT is significant: hundreds of enemies on screen, a full Zelda world to explore, and a budget that shows in every cutscene. Goal: Complete the tutorial, unlock two characters, use a Sync Strike in battle.

    Day 6
    Sunderfolk — palette cleanser

    Try Sunderfolk's first mission. Set up the smartphones, pick characters based on what sounds interesting (the salamander pyromancer is never a bad choice for a first run), and experience a fundamentally different pace. The tactical, turn-optional system will feel deliberately slow after Hyrule Warriors — that's the entire point. Board game night energy. There's no wrong way to play the first session; the goal is to see if the format resonates. Goal: Complete Mission 1. Understand one character's deck.

    Day 7
    Choose your direction

    By now, there's enough information to make an informed choice about where to go. The week has covered roguelite co-op in two different flavours, large-scale action co-op, and tactical board game co-op. Loved the roguelite loop? Risk of Rain Returns has hundreds more hours; TMNT has the Dimension Shellshock DLC. Want a story campaign? Sea of Stars is 30 hours of co-op RPG; Trine: Ultimate Collection is 28 hours of puzzle-platforming. Want infinite depth? Diablo III: Eternal Collection never runs out. Torment tiers go as deep as anyone wants to go.

    GameShare vs PlayStation Share Play

    GameShare on Switch 2 works differently from PlayStation's Share Play. Nintendo's implementation gives the second console a full local copy of the game for the duration of the session — not a streamed screen. The experience is identical on both consoles, with no latency gap.
