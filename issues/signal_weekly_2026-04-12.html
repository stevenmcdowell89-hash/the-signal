The Orbán Era on Trial

Hungarian parliamentary elections, April 12, 2026 — the tightest race Orbán has faced in 16 years. (Getty Images via NPR)
  • Viktor Orbán has won four consecutive parliamentary elections since 2010, each with a supermajority
  • Tisza party leader Péter Magyar is polling 7–9 points ahead going into today's vote
  • For the first time since 2006, a Hungarian opposition candidate has a credible path to a parliamentary majority

Today, polling stations across Hungary close at 7pm local time. Eight million registered voters — from a country of just under 10 million people — will deliver a verdict on whether one of Europe's most imitated nationalist templates has run its course.

Viktor Orbán has not merely been prime minister since 2010; he has systematically redesigned the institutions around him. Electoral boundaries were redrawn to maximise Fidesz's advantage from any given vote share. Public media became state media in all but name. The judiciary was packed and the independent press squeezed. The model was studied and admired — or condemned — across the continent.

The Challenger
  • Péter Magyar — Former insider, ex-husband of a Fidesz cabinet minister. His outsider turn came with a raw, viral speech that became a political phenomenon.
  • Tisza party — Centre-right, pro-EU, founded only two years ago. Now leading by as much as 19 points in independent polls.
  • Key vulnerability — To win a majority, Tisza needs the bonus seats that go to the largest party. Falling short of 50% risks a hung result.

What's different this time is the challenger. Péter Magyar is not the usual Western-liberal opposition figure that Orbán has painted as a foreign agent. He is a former insider — the ex-husband of a Fidesz justice minister — whose break with the establishment landed with an impact no one anticipated. The Tisza party he leads is less than two years old. It leads in every independent poll by margins that would produce a parliamentary majority if they hold.

The system, however, is built to resist. Hungary's electoral rules award a significant bloc of bonus seats to the plurality winner, meaning a close result could still produce a Fidesz majority. And Orbán's campaigning has been as disciplined as ever, painting the election as a choice between Hungarian sovereignty and Brussels-directed foreign interference — a frame he has used effectively four times before.

The Angle

Even if Tisza wins, the task of governing is enormous. Orbán has spent sixteen years remaking the state's architecture in his image — courts, media ownership, electoral rules, public broadcaster funding. Reversing that within a single parliamentary term, while managing a fractious coalition and a hostile bureaucracy, would test any government. The CFR's analysis this week makes a sobering point: winning is the easy part. What Hungary does next will matter for the entire continent's understanding of whether democratic backsliding is reversible.

Results are expected through Sunday evening. Counting begins immediately after polls close.

Todoist in 2026: The Features Worth Actually Using

Todoist has become one of the most copied productivity apps on Android — and yet most users run it like a basic to-do list when it's capable of significantly more. Three features that consistently surface in power-user threads as genuinely transformative:

Priority Filters + Favourites

The most effective Todoist setups use saved filters rather than projects as the daily view. A filter called "Today's Focus" — set to today & !p4 — shows your top-priority items only, stripping away the background noise. Add it as a favourite and it becomes your true daily dashboard.

Uncompletable Task Blocks

Prefix any task with * (asterisk + space) to make it uncompletable — it acts as a time block rather than a checkbox. Useful for mapping your day without over-scheduling: "* Deep work 10–12" sits alongside actual tasks and clarifies the day's shape without inflating your completion rate artificially.

The third underused feature: natural-language due dates with sections. Typing "#ProjectName /SectionName" while adding a task in Quick Add routes it directly to the right section, without opening a menu. For anyone managing several active projects, this alone reduces friction enough to justify relearning the input method.

Revolut Launches AIR — The Fintech AI Arms Race Gets Real

  • Revolut launched AIR (AI by Revolut) on April 9 — rolling out to UK customers first
  • Starling Bank introduced its own AI agent earlier this year; Monzo has similar functionality in development
  • All three neobanks have received regulatory fines in the last two years

Revolut's AIR is positioned as something beyond a chatbot. It's an embedded financial assistant that can pull spending breakdowns, track investment portfolios, manage subscriptions, block lost cards, and help plan travel budgets — all through a conversational interface, without navigating menus. The privacy positioning is deliberate: no user data is stored with third-party AI providers, and no training on customer transactions.

Revolut AIR

  • Launched April 9, UK-first rollout
  • Covers: spending insights, investments, subscriptions, card controls, travel budgeting, eSIM purchase
  • Zero data retention with external AI partners
  • No specific AI provider disclosed

Starling AI Agent

  • Launched earlier in 2026
  • Covers: account queries, spending categorisation, Spaces management
  • More conservative feature set at launch
  • Starling has stronger regulatory track record of the UK neobanks

The honest take: these features are most useful for people who don't already obsess over their finances — which is most people. If you're already using Monzo's Trends feature or Revolut's analytics tab, AIR's conversational wrapper is a nicer interface to the same data. If you're not actively managing your spending categories, it might be the nudge that actually makes the difference.

The Angle

The neobank AI race is less about the features themselves than about whether any of these companies can build the kind of trusted daily relationship that makes switching feel unthinkable. Revolut's international toolkit remains unmatched; Starling's business accounts are still the most serious in the market; Monzo's app is still the most refined. None of them has built the "everything app" yet. AIR is Revolut's best attempt at making the case that it can.

The Taper Paradox: Do Less, Run Faster

Taper week is the most misunderstood part of race preparation. (Adidas Running)
2.6%
Performance improvement from a properly executed taper — equivalent to more than 90 seconds off a 60-minute 10k

Three weeks out from a 10k race, the training instinct is to keep pushing. You feel fit, the legs feel good, and backing off feels like conceding ground you've fought to win. This is, according to a substantial body of research, completely backwards.

The science of tapering has become clearer over the last decade. A 2007 meta-analysis in Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, led by Laurent Bosquet, reviewed 182 studies across five endurance sports and produced a finding that still shapes how coaches think about race preparation: an exponential taper — cutting volume by 41–60% progressively over two weeks while holding intensity and frequency constant — produces measurable performance gains. For runners specifically, the optimal window is 8–14 days.

What's Actually Happening in a Taper

The gains are physiological, not psychological. During a training taper, the body replenishes glycogen stores depleted by hard training, repairs accumulated micro-tears in muscle tissue, bolsters the immune system (heavy training suppresses it in the short term), and — crucially — improves running economy: the oxygen cost of running at a given pace drops. You don't become less fit during a taper. You become more prepared to use the fitness you've already built.

10k Taper — 3-Week Framework
WeekVolumeIntensityFrequency
Week 3 (this week)~70–80% of peakHold — one threshold sessionSame as training
Week 2~50–60% of peakHold — one quality sessionSame as training
Race week~30–40% of peakOne short sharpener onlyReduce by 1 session

The critical word in all of this is volume. The taper cuts the length of sessions, not the number of them or the pace during quality work. A runner who backs off completely — no intervals, no tempo effort, just easy jogging — risks going flat. The body responds to race-pace stimuli; removing those stimuli entirely in the final two weeks leaves you well-rested but poorly primed.

— Thom Hunt, former American 10k record-holder, via Outside Online

Taper Anxiety Is Real (and Normal)

There's a recognised phenomenon in running called "taper tantrums" — a period of irritability, fatigue, and the profound sense that your fitness is evaporating. Studies confirm that these feelings are essentially the opposite of what's physiologically happening. Your legs may feel heavy as blood flow redirects and glycogen stores fill back up. Your aerobic capacity is not declining; it's consolidating.

The practical upshot for the final three weeks: cut total mileage significantly, keep at least one quality session per week at race effort (tempo intervals, not full-length threshold runs), don't add anything new (no new shoes, no experimental nutrition), and treat sleep and recovery as seriously as any training session. The fitness is already built. The taper is the process of letting it become race-ready.

Did You Know? Runners who aggressively taper for a 10k can expect to run their first kilometre more efficiently than at any point in their training — because glycogen stores are fully topped up and neuromuscular efficiency is at its peak. The race is the reward.
