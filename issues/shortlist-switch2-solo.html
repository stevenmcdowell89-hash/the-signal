There's a specific pleasure in discovering a game that fits perfectly around everything else in life — one that doesn't demand a six-hour unbroken session, that rewards the twenty-minute commute window and the post-bedtime hour equally. Switch 2's backwards compatibility doesn't just give you new hardware; it opens a back catalogue of some of the most distinctive games made in the past decade, many of which slipped quietly past during a busier season. This issue is a field guide to the ones worth going back for — and a couple arriving imminently.

The Lens — How We Picked These

What makes a solo game worth 100 hours of your life?

The Criteria
  • 📈 Meaningful progression — not just stats up; knowledge, mastery, or narrative momentum
  • 📖 Narrative or systemic depth — something to think about between sessions
  • ⏱ Tight pacing — no padding, no artificial gating, no twenty-hour tutorials
  • 🎲 Right length — 15-25 hour sweet spot, or roguelites with genuine run-to-run variety
  • ✅ Available now or imminently on Switch 2 (native or backwards compatible)

The question driving this issue is simple: what would you play after Blue Prince? Not as a palate cleanser — as a worthy successor. Blue Prince works because it understands something most games don't: the only thing worth giving the player is information. Every run teaches you something. Every failure is a step toward an answer you didn't even know existed. That's a high bar.

The games on this list were evaluated against five criteria. First, progression must mean something beyond a number going up — it should feel like accumulating mastery, uncovering a mystery, or building towards a genuinely climactic moment. Second, the game needs either narrative depth (a reason to care about its world and characters) or systemic depth (a ruleset complex enough to generate emergent stories). These aren't mutually exclusive; the best games on this list have both.

Third, pacing. Games that front-load twenty hours of setup before becoming interesting didn't make the cut. Switch 2 is a handheld — it gets played in thirty-minute windows on trains and during lunch. Every game here earns your attention from the first session. Fourth, the length has to be honest: either a defined 15-25 hour experience that doesn't overstay its welcome, or a roguelite with enough genuine variety that run 40 feels different from run 1. Finally, everything here is playable on Switch 2 today or in the very near future.

Did You Know

According to the NPD Group, only 17% of players who start an open-world RPG reach its conclusion. Games with tighter 15-25 hour runtimes consistently post completion rates three to four times higher — which means more people actually finish the story you paid for.

Caves of Qud

This is the Morrowind of roguelikes — not in setting, but in the way it treats you as a genuine participant in its world. After seventeen years of development, Caves of Qud reached 1.0 in late 2024 and came to Switch in early 2026. What you get is a post-apocalyptic sci-fantasy world of extraordinary depth, procedurally generated but stuffed with authored content: factions, histories, ancient ruins, cities that remember what happened to them.

Character creation alone is a commitment. Choose between mutated humans — who can start the game with a beak, slime glands, gelatinous anatomy, or the ability to absorb nearby minerals — or True Kin, the cybernetically enhanced remnants of an ancient civilisation who can slot in night-vision implants, rapid release wrists, and electromagnetic pulse fields. Every combination plays differently. Every death teaches you something about the world's internal logic.

The writing is the thing. Hugo Award-winning fiction authors have contributed to the world-building. The procedural quest generation means no two playthroughs surface identical stories. For anyone who wants a game that genuinely surprises them a hundred hours in, nothing on this list competes.

Inscryption

You wake up in a dark cabin. Across the table sits a hooded figure called Leshy. He wants you to play cards. This is either straightforward or the most elaborate deception in the history of the medium — and within two hours, you'll know exactly which.

Inscryption is a deckbuilder with escape-room puzzles between rounds. The card mechanics are genuinely excellent — sacrificing weak cards to summon powerful beasts, managing tooth and bone as resources, building synergies around animal totems. But the game's real move is structural. Between rounds, you can stand up from the table and explore the cabin. There are items on shelves. Codes to find. Secrets that feed back into the card game in ways that initially seem impossible.

Then the game reinvents itself. Once. And again. Each reinvention maintains the deckbuilding DNA while completely overhauling the aesthetic, tone, and mechanics around it. Ten hours. Not a minute wasted. If you're the kind of person who found Blue Prince's genre-defying structure thrilling, Inscryption was made for you.

Why It's Here

Daniel Mullins built Inscryption as a solo developer. Its construction — layered secrets, a game-within-a-game-within-a-game — is a remarkable piece of design craft. At £16 for ten dense hours, the value proposition is absurd.

"What happens when the only thing you keep between lives is what you know?"

Outer Wilds: Archaeologist Edition

Every 22 minutes, the sun explodes. The solar system resets. You start over. What you carry forward is not better equipment, not upgraded stats — only the knowledge of what you discovered in the previous loop. An ancient alien civilization called the Nomai left records of their research throughout the solar system, and piece by piece, you reconstruct what they were trying to do and why it matters right now.

The Archaeologist Edition includes Echoes of the Eye, a DLC expansion that takes the game's central mechanic in a completely different, deeply unsettling direction. Base game alone runs 12-15 hours; with DLC you're looking at 20-25. Both deserve full attention.

This is the purest possible expression of the "knowledge as progression" philosophy. Every planet in the solar system has its own physics, its own secrets, its own logic. A moon with sand that flows upward. A planet with a shrinking surface. An ash-covered world orbiting too close to the sun. None of these are puzzles in the traditional sense — they're places to understand. When you finally understand them, the game ends, and the ending is one of the best in all of interactive fiction. The Switch 2 port holds a solid 30fps via backwards compatibility.

13 Sentinels: Aegis Rim

Quick Stats
GenreVisual novel + RTS hybrid
Price~£35 / $40
Playtime30–35 hours
RPG Site Score100/100

Thirteen protagonists, multiple time periods, one alien invasion — and Vanillaware's most ambitious storytelling yet. RPG Site awarded it a perfect score, and that's not hyperbole. The narrative structure is genuinely complex: you unravel the story non-linearly, switching between characters who each hold a different piece of the puzzle, and the moment it clicks together is extraordinary.

The RTS battles are a pleasant surprise — not a distraction from the visual novel sections, but a natural extension of them. You manage mech loadouts and cooldown rotations, cycle your pilots to prevent fatigue, and choose your engagement priorities under genuine time pressure. Don't let the anime aesthetic fool you: the tactical layer is real. And Vanillaware's hand-drawn art is among the best in the medium.

Sources: Nintendo Life (9/10) · RPG Site (100/100)

"Its narrative complexity rivals Baldur's Gate 3 while asking for a fraction of the time."

The case for 13 Sentinels is this: it's a game where the storytelling is the innovation. Not the combat system, not the art (though both are excellent) — the structure of how it delivers its story across thirteen intersecting arcs is something that hasn't been done quite like this anywhere else.

  • Turn-based positional combat — manoeuvre enemies into each other's gunfire
  • John Wick energy with Into the Breach tactical depth
  • Multiple difficulty tiers — your first run is the tutorial

    • Fights in Tight Spaces

    The pitch is John Wick meets Into the Breach, and it delivers on both halves. A minimalist white-and-grey aesthetic — Superhot adjacent — stages every encounter on a small grid. You play cards to punch, kick, shove, and redirect; enemies telegraph their next moves with coloured indicators; the whole thing becomes a spatial puzzle about positioning, momentum, and not leaving yourself flanked.

    The tactical depth accumulates quietly. By the time you've built a deck around combo chains and environmental hazards — kicking enemies off ledges, shoving them into each other, burning your entire hand on one devastating sequence — you realise this is a game about geometry as much as cards. At £18 for a first run plus hours of mastery depth, it's one of the better-value entries on this list.

    Why This Is Here

    Wildermyth is the rare game where the procedural storytelling actually works. Your heroes age. They fall in love. They can permanently fuse with forest spirits, becoming part-tree. Children inherit the legacies of retired parents. No two campaigns are identical, and the emergent stories it generates are genuinely moving — something no amount of hand-crafted cutscenes can guarantee.

    "What if your RPG characters wrote their own stories?"

    Wildermyth

    Imagine XCOM built inside a tabletop RPG by a storytelling engine that genuinely cares about its characters. Wildermyth generates your heroes procedurally — their backstories, their personalities, the relationships they form during campaigns — then puts them on a grid-based tactical battlefield where the combat is clean, fast, and built around magical infusion and environmental manipulation.

    The headline mechanic is transformation: heroes can merge with forest spirits, becoming part-animal, part-plant, part-something-else, permanently changing their combat abilities and visual appearance. They can retire to legend, and their descendants inherit echoes of their power. The Switch port has stability issues as of April 2026 — occasional crashes and a reported save corruption bug — but patches are in progress, and when it runs cleanly, it's unlike anything else available on the platform.

    Best suited to fans of FTL's emergent storytelling and Fire Emblem's character attachment. If losing a soldier and genuinely feeling something about it appeals to you, Wildermyth is the game.

    Sources: GodisaGeek review · Digitally Downloaded

    Why This Is Here

    Rogue Legacy 2 is the argument that meta-progression, done well, is its own form of storytelling. Fifteen distinct character classes, each with genuinely different mechanics. A trait system so varied that your next heir might have IBS (which causes fog of war around food pickups), gigantism (doubled size, halved doors), or colour blindness (the world rendered in greyscale). These aren't jokes — they're mechanical constraints that force you to play differently every run.

    Rogue Legacy 2

    Each death passes the torch to a new heir — a descendant of your previous character, complete with their own randomly assigned traits, class, and backstory fragments. The estate that grows between runs is your legacy: new biomes unlocked, new classes discovered, heirlooms passed down that permanently expand your toolkit.

    Unlike Dead Cells — which is a superb combat game that happens to be a roguelite — Rogue Legacy 2 makes the between-run investment feel meaningful. Your first successful run, which will take most players 16-20 hours, is essentially the tutorial. The real game begins when you're chasing alternate endings, completing class-specific challenges, and hunting the late-game content that opens after the credits roll.

    At £22 for 30-80 hours of content, with an ENEBA score of 9.9/10 and a recommendation from Nintendo Life's best-roguelikes roundup, it earns its place here. The Switch version runs excellently — fast, fluid, no performance concerns.

    The Aside — A Pattern

    The best solo games make failure interesting

    In Hades, dying is how you learn Zagreus's backstory. Failure is the narrative engine, not a setback.

    Look across this shortlist and you'll notice a pattern. Caves of Qud kills you in ways that teach you what the world's rules actually are. Outer Wilds's 22-minute loops are not failures — they're research sessions. Inscryption weaponises your confusion about what it even is. Rogue Legacy 2 converts defeat into dynasty-building. Fights in Tight Spaces punishes bad positioning so immediately that you understand what went wrong before the death screen finishes loading.

    Hades made this explicit: dying was literally the point, because every conversation with the underworld's inhabitants advanced the story. FTL made it mathematical — defeat reveals your decision tree's flaw, and the next run you route differently. The best roguelites and loop-based games have understood for years what linear games struggle with: the space around failure is where players actually think. Make that space interesting and you have a game that teaches rather than frustrates. Every pick on this list passes that test.

    The UE5 remake is confirmed for Switch 2 in 2026. No release date yet. The original Oblivion — with its absurd NPC schedules, its hand-crafted Daedric quests, and its peculiar elder-statesmen energy — is one of the great open-world time sinks. If the remaster preserves the soul of it (and doesn't smooth away all the weirdness in the name of modernity), this will be an event.

    Coming to Switch 2 in 2026 TBC. The original Moonlighter had a clever loop — dungeon-dive for loot, return to sell it in your shop, reinvest in gear.
