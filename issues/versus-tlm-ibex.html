This issue started as a simple question: given you're already on Ibex, is there any reason to look at The Lyss Method? The answer is more nuanced than either camp's marketing suggests. Dr. Alyssa Olenick is one of the most credible voices in hybrid training. Ryan Fischer built something that works. The Signal is here to figure out which one actually fits a specific goal — a 10K in May, a holiday in June, a squat that keeps going up.

Every programme makes promises. The good ones keep them for the right person. The Lyss Method (TLM) and Ibex Training both target the hybrid athlete — someone who lifts and runs and refuses to fully sacrifice one for the other. But they come from different philosophies, different price points, and different ideas of what a hybrid programme actually looks like.

App Experience: The Unglamorous Factor

No one buys a fitness programme for the app. But they do quit because of it. Ibex runs a custom app with built-in tracking and video demos that users consistently describe as clean and intuitive. TLM runs on Everfit — a third-party platform — and feedback is mixed. The recurring complaint: the app is rigid about day assignment. Miss a scheduled run and it logs as "missed run 1" even if you're intentionally rescheduling. You can't feed in watch metrics from a Garmin. For someone who tracks everything on a Garmin Venu X1, that's friction every single week.

Quick Spec: App Features
  • Ibex App — Custom iOS/Android, built-in exercise tracking, video demos, movement swaps. Users report straightforward UX.
  • TLM (Everfit) — Third-party platform; coaching messaging, resource library, video feedback groups. Day-locking is the main reported frustration.
  • Garmin integration — Neither integrates natively with Garmin Connect. Ibex slightly better for manual flexibility.

The Seasons Philosophy: Why It's Different

Most hybrid programmes ask you to do everything at once, every week, indefinitely. The Lyss Method explicitly rejects this. The "seasons approach" periodises between phases: a strength-focused block, an endurance-focused block, a true hybrid block. The logic is grounded in research on concurrent training — specifically the interference effect, which shows that high volumes of strength and endurance training compete for adaptive resources at the cellular level.

This matters for someone targeting a 10K on May 3rd. You cannot simultaneously maximise squat strength and 10K race fitness. One of them leads; the other supports. TLM is designed around that truth. Right now, you're in pre-race territory — which means you'd be on PERFORM (or potentially RACE if you switch). Post-race in mid-May through June, you'd be in an ideal window for a strength-focused phase before the Netherlands holiday. TLM is built for this cycle.

"The goal of PERFORM is not to be the best lifting programme or the best running programme. It's to be the programme that keeps both going without either one imploding."

— The Lyss Method programming philosophy, doclyssfitness.com

PERFORM: What the Sessions Actually Look Like

PERFORM is the flagship hybrid track. Three sessions per week, approximately 60 minutes each. The movement patterns are orthodox — squats, deadlifts, presses, rows as the main lifts, with single-leg work, core, and athletic movements in the accessory slots. Progressive overload is applied to main movements across 8–12 weeks; accessories rotate every 4–6 weeks to manage fatigue and maintain stimulus.

The RPE/RIR prescription system is worth highlighting. Every working set has an effort target — "4×6 @ RPE 7-8" tells you not just what to do but how hard to work. For someone wearing a Garmin and already thinking about training readiness, this is a natural fit. You adjust based on how you feel that day, and the programme gives you the framework to do that intelligently.

The RACE Track: The Ace Card

No programme in this comparison has an equivalent to TLM's RACE track. It's an 18-week race-specific programme that deliberately tapers lifting volume as running volume climbs. The architecture is designed for athletes who want to arrive at a race without having sacrificed 12 weeks of gym progress to get there. The lifting doesn't disappear — it steps back into a maintenance role while running takes the wheel.

For a 10K target on May 3rd, you're currently inside that race window. PERFORM keeps both fires burning at moderate intensity. If you were starting fresh with a TLM subscription, you might also consider triggering RACE now and using the intelligent taper to hit May 3rd in better running shape, knowing the lifting picks back up in earnest in mid-May.

  • Best for: athletes with a specific race date and a gym habit to protect

The Evidence Base

The Lyss Method isn't just citing exercise science for credibility — it's built by someone who researches concurrent training specifically. Dr. Olenick's academic background gives the programme something most alternatives can't claim: a theoretical framework that maps onto what the research actually says about lifting and running together. That doesn't guarantee it produces better results in practice, but it does mean the decisions (why this loading scheme, why this rep range, why this sequence) have defensible answers.

Did You Know?

The "interference effect" in concurrent training is most pronounced when high-volume endurance work precedes strength training in the same session. TLM's PERFORM structure separates sessions entirely to minimise this — your running days and lifting days don't compete for the same recovery window. Stronger By Science on concurrent training

Where TLM Falls Short

The price is real. At $89/month — roughly £70 — TLM is a premium product. There's no annual discount that brings it meaningfully closer to Ibex. The Everfit app experience is a legitimate friction point for organised, data-driven athletes. If you're tracking every session in Garmin Connect and want to cross-reference, the inability to feed in watch metrics is a daily annoyance.

There's also a community concern surfaced in Reddit reports: at least one user's friend trained for a marathon with TLM and walked the last 8+ miles — reportedly due to insufficient running volume in the plan. That's a single anecdote, but it echoes a broader tension: TLM is brilliant at protecting lifting during run builds. There's less evidence it's built for athletes whose primary goal is race performance over gym performance.

"At $89/month, TLM is asking you to pay for a worldview as much as a programme. The worldview is sound. Whether it's yours to pay for depends on what you're optimising for."

Ibex Training arrived from CrossFit culture but evolved into something more useful: a structured hybrid programme with the metabolic conditioning of functional fitness and the progressive overload logic of powerbuilding. Lift/Run is the flagship hybrid track, and for three-days-a-week gym athletes who also run, it's exceptionally well-designed.

Lift/Run: What It Actually Is

Where TLM PERFORM gives you three lifting-centric sessions with running plans alongside them, Ibex Lift/Run integrates both explicitly. The typical week is three full-body strength days plus three run days — but crucially, the structure is flexible. You can compress it to three days or extend to five. The full-body approach on lifting days is a deliberate choice: by spreading leg volume across three sessions rather than hammering it once per week, the programme keeps the legs fresher for running. That's not accidental — it's the same concurrent training logic TLM uses, but implemented structurally rather than through seasons periodisation.

Typical Lift/Run Week
  • Monday — Lift + optional easy run
  • Tuesday — Interval run
  • Wednesday — Lift + optional conditioning
  • Thursday — Easy run
  • Friday — Lift + optional aerobic machine
  • Saturday — Long run or Hyrox gym day
  • Sunday — Rest

The running component has three difficulty levels to choose from — so whether you're managing 30km weeks or just keeping the aerobic base ticking over during a heavy lifting block, there's a lane for you. Threshold intervals are built in, influenced by Hyrox-style conditioning. The result is a programme that genuinely improves running performance, not just lifter-who-jogs performance.

The 4-week block structure with rotating exercises is a practical solution to a real problem: programme monotony. Accessory work changes every four weeks, which keeps adaptation stimulus fresh without requiring the athlete to constantly design their own variation. The compounds — bench, squat, deadlift — progress in linear fashion with percentage-based or rep-max loading. The structure is transparent and simple enough that you understand what's happening week to week.

Performance Aesthetics: The Overlooked Track

The conversation about Ibex tends to centre on Lift/Run, but Performance Aesthetics is a serious programme worth understanding, especially for someone who wants to prioritise hypertrophy during the summer (post-10K, post-holiday). Six days per week, 45–60 minutes — a push/pull/leg split with conditioning day, full-body heavy day, and full-body density day. Users report significant strength PRs on back squat, deadlift, and strict press. The four built-in core circuits and optional metcon pieces mean conditioning never fully disappears.

The ability to pivot from Lift/Run to Performance Aesthetics within the same subscription is a significant practical advantage. After the 10K in May and ahead of the Netherlands holiday in June, a five-to-six-week block of PFA — leaning into body composition — becomes an obvious move. TLM's equivalent would be switching to BUILD, but you're paying $89/month either way. With Ibex, this pivot costs nothing.

The Value Equation

$33/month with frequent sales — the annual option can drop to around $237/year, which works out to under $20/month. That's not a budget compromise. Ibex at full price is a good deal; Ibex on sale is an embarrassingly good deal. The programme depth at that price point has no serious competition: 22+ distinct programmes, a custom app, video demos, and a Facebook community.

The absence of direct coaching is the trade-off. Ibex has a community, not coaches. If you want someone with credentials to review your form, adjust your loading, or answer programme questions with a PhD-level response, Ibex doesn't offer that. For an experienced gym-goer who knows the lifts and understands progressive overload, this rarely matters. For someone earlier in their training journey, it's a meaningful gap.

LIFT/RUN vs PFA — When to Use Each

  • Lift/Run now (April): 10K on May 3 — running volume is the priority. Full-body lifting preserves strength without burying the legs before race day.
  • PFA post-race (May–June): 10K is done, Netherlands trip is 8 weeks out. Hypertrophy window — PFA's 6-day structure hits body composition hard.

The Verdict

Ibex wins. TLM earns its price for one specific thing.

The finish line is the context that changes everything. Photo: Unsplash
What does it actually take for a $89/month programme to beat a $33/month one that already works?

This comparison has a clear winner and a clear asterisk. The asterisk matters.

Ibex Training

For the current context — 10K prep, post-race hypertrophy, full gym access, existing familiarity — Ibex is the right call. The price gap alone would need to be substantially overcome by TLM to justify the switch. It isn't, in this case.

Why Ibex Wins Here

The current situation is straightforward: you have a 10K on May 3rd, a holiday on June 30th, and you're already on Ibex. Lift/Run is structurally sound for concurrent training. The running component is integrated and competent. The full-body approach protects running legs. Performance Aesthetics is waiting in the wings for the post-race body composition push. None of this requires $89/month to execute.

The programme breadth advantage matters too. In the next 12 weeks alone, the plan is: 10K prep → race → 4-6 weeks of body composition work → holiday. Ibex handles that entire arc within one subscription: Lift/Run now, Performance Aesthetics from mid-May, IBEX60 or a holiday maintenance block in June. TLM does the same — PERFORM or RACE now, BUILD from May — but at 2.7× the cost, for a user who already knows the Ibex system and has no learning curve to overcome.

Ibex — Stay Here If:

  • You have a race within 12 weeks and want structured but not over-complicated prep
  • Hypertrophy is an equal or greater goal to running performance
  • Programme switching (Lift/Run → PFA → IBEX60) is appealing
  • You track on Garmin and want app flexibility, not day-locking
  • The price gap is a real factor in what you're willing to spend

TLM — Switch If:

  • Running performance becomes the primary goal — half-marathon, marathon, or ultra aspirations
  • You want coaching access, not just community
  • The seasons periodisation model is what your training has been missing
  • You want structured concurrent training designed by someone who specifically researches it
  • Price is not a deciding factor and you want the premium product

The Asterisk: When TLM Wins

If the 10K becomes a half-marathon, the half becomes a marathon, and the running goal starts to meaningfully lead — TLM RACE becomes the most defensible race-prep infrastructure available at any price in this category. The seasons model and the intelligent lift-taper are genuinely superior to anything Ibex offers for serious run prioritisation. The PhD-level concurrent training design also matters more as distances increase, where the interference effect becomes more aggressive and more consequential.

TLM also wins on coaching. If your squat form is breaking down, your running gait is concerning you, or you have a specific problem you want answered by someone with a doctorate in exercise physiology, Ibex's Facebook community is a structural limitation. TLM's direct coaching access is a real premium service for a specific kind of athlete.

"Ibex is the right answer for right now. TLM is the right answer for a different version of this goal — one where the running distance doubles and the finish line matters more than the squat rack."

— The Signal Verdict
