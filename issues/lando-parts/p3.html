The Orbán Era on Trial

Hungarian parliamentary elections, April 12, 2026 — the tightest race Orbán has faced in 16 years. (Getty Images via NPR)
  • Viktor Orbán has won four consecutive parliamentary elections since 2010, each with a supermajority
  • Tisza party leader Péter Magyar is polling 7–9 points ahead going into today's vote
  • For the first time since 2006, a Hungarian opposition candidate has a credible path to a parliamentary majority

Today, polling stations across Hungary close at 7pm local time. Eight million registered voters — from a country of just under 10 million people — will deliver a verdict on whether one of Europe's most imitated nationalist templates has run its course.

Viktor Orbán has not merely been prime minister since 2010; he has systematically redesigned the institutions around him. Electoral boundaries were redrawn to maximise Fidesz's advantage from any given vote share. Public media became state media in all but name. The judiciary was packed and the independent press squeezed. The model was studied and admired — or condemned — across the continent.

The Challenger
  • Péter Magyar — Former insider, ex-husband of a Fidesz cabinet minister. His outsider turn came with a raw, viral speech that became a political phenomenon.
  • Tisza party — Centre-right, pro-EU, founded only two years ago. Now leading by as much as 19 points in independent polls.
  • Key vulnerability — To win a majority, Tisza needs the bonus seats that go to the largest party. Falling short of 50% risks a hung result.

What's different this time is the challenger. Péter Magyar is not the usual Western-liberal opposition figure that Orbán has painted as a foreign agent. He is a former insider — the ex-husband of a Fidesz justice minister — whose break with the establishment landed with an impact no one anticipated. The Tisza party he leads is less than two years old. It leads in every independent poll by margins that would produce a parliamentary majority if they hold.

The system, however, is built to resist. Hungary's electoral rules award a significant bloc of bonus seats to the plurality winner, meaning a close result could still produce a Fidesz majority. And Orbán's campaigning has been as disciplined as ever, painting the election as a choice between Hungarian sovereignty and Brussels-directed foreign interference — a frame he has used effectively four times before.

The Angle

Even if Tisza wins, the task of governing is enormous. Orbán has spent sixteen years remaking the state's architecture in his image — courts, media ownership, electoral rules, public broadcaster funding. Reversing that within a single parliamentary term, while managing a fractious coalition and a hostile bureaucracy, would test any government. The CFR's analysis this week makes a sobering point: winning is the easy part. What Hungary does next will matter for the entire continent's understanding of whether democratic backsliding is reversible.

Results are expected through Sunday evening. Counting begins immediately after polls close.

Hours of direct US–Iran talks this weekend

The most significant development this week: the first direct US–Iranian talks in over a decade, held in Islamabad, Pakistan, with Pakistani PM Shehbaz Sharif acting as host and facilitator. Senior delegations met for more than 21 hours across two days.

The ceasefire: A two-week ceasefire brokered by Pakistan came into effect April 8, after Trump issued an extraordinary threat to demolish "every bridge and power plant in Iran" within 24 hours. Hours before the deadline, Iran accepted a modified 10-point peace framework. Brent crude, which had spiked to $117/barrel, fell more than 16% in a single session on the news.

The talks: JD Vance led the US delegation. After 21 hours of negotiation, Vance emerged to say no deal had been reached — "Iran has chosen not to accept our terms" — but confirmed that the US's "final and best offer" remained on the table. Pakistan called the talks a "stepping stone." Iran's Supreme National Security Council remained publicly hardline.

The Strait: US warships have begun transiting the Strait of Hormuz for the first time since hostilities began. Three supertankers exited the Gulf via a "trial anchorage" corridor on April 11. Iran continues to insist it controls the waterway's management — limiting traffic to 15 vessels per day — and warns mines remain outside designated routes.

China dimension: US intelligence agencies assessed this week that China may have approved a shipment of shoulder-fired missiles to Iran. Beijing denied it. The alleged Chinese move would represent a major escalation and complicates Trump's planned visit to Beijing next month.

Lebanon front: Fighting between Israeli forces and Hezbollah continues despite the ceasefire framework. The IDF encircled Bint Jbeil and reported killing approximately 200 Hezbollah fighters on April 8. Israel and Lebanon are expected to begin US-brokered direct negotiations in Washington next week — but Israel insists Hezbollah disarmament is non-negotiable.

Pope Leo made an impassioned appeal from St Peter's Basilica on Saturday: "Stop! It is time for peace!" — the first US Pope's highest-profile intervention in the conflict.
