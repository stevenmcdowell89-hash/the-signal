Weeks Since War Began
Week 6
of the US-Israel-Iran war — and the strategic consequences are accelerating faster than the battlefield

Six weeks in, the 2026 Iran war has stopped being a regional crisis and started being a civilisational stress-test. This week produced three developments that analysts will be writing about for years: Iran's parliament openly debating withdrawal from the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty; Trump threatening to pull the US out of NATO after European allies refused to join military operations to reopen the Strait of Hormuz; and Iran shooting down a US F-15E Strike Eagle — the first confirmed loss of a US combat aircraft of that type to hostile fire in decades.

The bridge strike on Thursday was the moment that crystallised the week's character. The B1 suspension bridge west of Tehran — a civilian infrastructure project with no military function — was hit by US-Israeli airstrikes, then struck again an hour later as emergency crews arrived. Eight people died, 95 were injured. Iran's Red Crescent put total civilian infrastructure strikes at over 113,000 sites since the war began. Whether those numbers are accurate or not, the double-tap on rescue workers is the kind of moment that reshapes international opinion in ways that outlast the conflict itself.

Key Pressures This Week
  • Hormuz — UK convened 40+ nations without US presence
  • NPT — Iran's parliament considering exit from nuclear treaty
  • NATO — Trump called it a "paper tiger"; Hegseth declined to confirm Article 5
  • Oil — OPEC March output fell sharply; Taiwan increasing fuel surcharges 157%
  • Ukraine — Russia's March gains: just 23km², lowest since 2023

Iran's response has been methodical rather than panicked. Drone and missile attacks on Kuwait's oil infrastructure, Qatar's LNG tankers, Amazon Web Services servers in Bahrain, and — in a move that felt almost theatrical — a reported strike on an Oracle data centre in Dubai (which UAE officials disputed). The message wasn't the damage; it was the target selection. Iran is demonstrating that it can reach anything in the Gulf.

The Strait of Hormuz situation deserves its own paragraph. Around 20% of the world's oil passes through those 21 nautical miles of water. Iran and Oman are reportedly drafting a "maritime transit protocol" — which is either a genuine off-ramp or a mechanism for Iran to exert permanent leverage over global energy supply. Britain hosting 40+ countries to discuss reopening the strait, with the US conspicuously absent after Trump declared it "not America's job," is the kind of diplomatic inversion that would have seemed like a Tom Clancy plot twist two years ago.

The Angle

The most important story isn't the missiles. It's NATO. The alliance has survived disagreements over burden-sharing, over Iraq, over Afghanistan. It has never faced a US president who launched a war without consulting allies, then demanded they fight in it, then called the alliance a "paper tiger" when they refused. If Trump follows through on withdrawal rhetoric, the entire post-1945 European security architecture shifts on its axis. Russia is watching. China is watching. The Iran war may end; what it has done to Western institutional trust will not repair quickly.

Ukraine: Zelensky Proposes Easter Ceasefire, Russia Strikes Markets

  • Russian drone struck a market in Nikopol this week, killing 5, wounding 19
  • Russia fired 280+ drones overnight — 260 intercepted according to Ukrainian Air Force
  • Russia's March territorial gains: just 23km² — a fraction of January's 319km²
  • Zelensky proposed Easter ceasefire including mutual halt to energy infrastructure strikes; Kremlin rejected it

The contrast between Russia's narrative of battlefield progress and the ISW's data is striking. After two and a half years of war, March 2026 was Russia's worst month for territorial gains since the conflict began. Ukraine's counter-strikes in the south have disrupted logistics and supply chains in ways that are starting to show in measurable outcomes — not spectacular breakthroughs, but grinding attrition that is working. The Easter ceasefire rejection from Moscow was predictable; what's notable is Zelensky's continued willingness to propose restraint on energy infrastructure, which costs him politically at home.

Steam Machine: Still Coming, Just Not Q1

Valve has reaffirmed its 2026 commitment to the Steam Machine — a console-form mini PC with 16GB DDR5 RAM and 8GB GDDR6 VRAM — after RAM supply concerns raised fears of a further delay. "Nothing has actually changed," the company told The Verge. The original "early 2026" window has clearly slipped, but the H1 target still appears to be the goal. This matters: if you're currently using GeForce NOW to play on a Xiaomi Pad, the Steam Machine represents a different calculus — local hardware, no subscription, proper desktop experience. Worth watching the pricing announcement carefully.

Google Gemini Gets Personal (Literally)

Google's most significant Gemini update of the year has been quietly rolling out: Personal Intelligence, which connects Gemini to your Gmail, Photos, Drive, and Search history to deliver contextualised, personalised responses rather than generic chat. It's currently US-only for Pro/Ultra subscribers, but a global rollout is expected this year. The practical upshot — Gemini summarising long email threads, prioritising your inbox, drafting with your tone — is genuinely useful in a way that previous AI assistant promises weren't. The privacy architecture matters here: opt-in only, each app connected individually.

+6

The 2025-26 Serie A title race is over in everything but mathematical confirmation. Inter's 69 points after 30 games represents one of the dominant campaigns in recent memory — 22 wins from 30 games, +42 goal difference. Milan have closed to within six points and Napoli are seven back, but Inter's form and fixture list give them almost no room to stumble. The more interesting subplot is fourth place: Como's extraordinary season under Cesc Fàbregas has them at 57 points, ahead of Juventus (54) and level-points Roma. The FA Cup Did Something Beautiful Yesterday

Arsenal went to Southampton as 70-point Premier League leaders, nine points clear at the top, and lost 2-1 to a Championship club. Seventh in the second division. Southampton, managed by Tonda Eckert, had Ross Stewart score in the 35th minute — Ben White misjudging a long ball — and then substitute Shea Charles seal it in the 85th with a finish that will be replayed for years. Viktor Gyökeres equalised for Arsenal on 68 minutes, and for a moment normal service looked likely. Then Charles happened.

Meanwhile at the Etihad, Erling Haaland scored a hat-trick in 18 minutes to put Manchester City 4-0 up against Liverpool. Haaland's 12th hat-trick since joining City in 2022. Salah missed a penalty in the 64th minute. The full story of that afternoon — Arsenal out, Liverpool out, Southampton to Wembley — is one of the cleanest narratives the FA Cup has produced in decades. A Championship side, at Wembley, against Manchester City. You could not write it.

The Take

Arsenal are still nine points clear in the league and heading into the Champions League quarter-final against Sporting CP on Tuesday. The cup exits hurt, but the real prize is still in front of them. The more pressing question is Arteta's squad depth — Rice and Saka were absent yesterday, Gabriel limped off. The CL draw against Leverkusen showed they can manage without their best, but Tuesday is a different kind of pressure.

Star Wars Returns. Tonight.

Star Wars: Maul – Shadow Lord premieres on Disney+ tonight, 6 April 2026
What happens when the franchise's most visually compelling villain finally gets his own show?

Star Wars: Maul – Shadow Lord premieres on Disney+ tonight, and it's the project that has had the franchise's animated fanbase more genuinely excited than anything since The Clone Wars finale. Set after the Clone Wars, it follows Maul rebuilding his criminal syndicate on a planet the Empire hasn't yet touched — and, crucially, finding a disillusioned young Jedi Padawan who could become his apprentice. Early critical word (screeners went out last week) is very positive.

April Is Fantasy Fiction's Strongest Month in Years

This is the first appearance of The Shelf, so there's no catch-up gap to cover — we're starting fresh. And as a first issue, April couldn't be a better month to launch it: the fantasy and sci-fi calendar is genuinely stacked, with a Hugo/Nebula winner debuting his first novel, James S.A. Corey continuing his post-Expanse space opera, and a T.J. Klune standalone arriving for fans of his warmer, more literary fantasy voice.

Books

The Subtle Art of Folding Space

John Chu · Tordotcom · 7 April

John Chu has already won the Hugo for Best Short Story and the Nebula for Best Novelette. This is his debut novel. For a writer whose shorter work is known for precision and emotional intelligence, the step up to long form is the year's most interesting experiment in genre fiction. Details are sparse by design — Tordotcom is letting the author's reputation do the work.

The Faith of Beasts

James S.A. Corey · Orbit · 14 April

The Expanse authors continue The Captive's War — their post-Expanse space opera trilogy about humanity enslaved by the alien Carryx empire. Book 2 raises the stakes as an agent of the Swarm infiltrates the Carryx war machine. If you're an Expanse fan who hasn't started this trilogy, it has all the same structural strengths: massive-scale conflict, character-driven plotting, genuine stakes. No Malazan spoilers in sight — different universe entirely.

City of Iron and Ivy

Thomas Kent West · Forever · 21 April

Alternate-history Victorian London where botanical magic is real — and someone is using it to kill. Murder mystery with industrial-era grit, living dangerous plant sorcery, and a body count. The pitch sounds niche but the execution, from advance reviews, is exceptional. You Can Lift and Run. The Science Says Stop Worrying.

Concurrent training research — Barbell Medicine

The "interference effect" is one of fitness's most persistent myths: the idea that combining strength training and cardio will blunt your gains. It has enough truth in it to sound credible, but not enough to make it a practical concern for most people training for health and body composition rather than elite performance.

Barbell Medicine published the most comprehensive layperson breakdown of the evidence recently, and the conclusion is clear: three recent meta-analyses found that concurrent training does not compromise strength or hypertrophy gains significantly when compared to lifting-only programmes. The interference effect, when it does appear, is primarily about explosive power — not the kind of strength or muscle mass you're building through a structured programme like Ibex.

0
Studies in the key 2021 meta-analysis on concurrent training — finding no strength impairment for moderate-level athletes

The mechanism most people worry about — AMPK (activated by cardio) blocking mTOR (activated by lifting) — is real at the cellular level, but the long-term evidence suggests this transient suppression doesn't translate into meaningful differences in muscle growth over weeks and months. The practical concern isn't the molecular pathway; it's total training load exceeding recovery capacity. That's the actual interference. Add too much too fast in either direction and something gives.

Practical Rules for Concurrent Training
Rule Why It Matters
Lift before you run (same day) Residual fatigue from cardio reduces motor unit recruitment in strength work. Lift first for maximal neuromuscular stimulus.
6+ hours between sessions Separating sessions by 6+ hours minimises acute AMPK/mTOR competition. Different-day training eliminates the concern entirely.
Add cardio gradually The interference is mostly about total load exceeding recovery — not the concurrent nature itself. Build running volume at 10% per week max.
Zone 2 over HIIT High-intensity cardio creates more glycolytic fatigue and stronger AMPK activation. Moderate-intensity continuous training (Zone 2) minimises interference with strength adaptation.
Watch your Garmin metrics Training Readiness below 50 after a run day is a signal, not a suggestion. Body Battery under 30 at training time means reduce intensity regardless of schedule.

Reading Your Garmin Data During Concurrent Training

For someone running the Ibex 3-day programme alongside recreational running, the Garmin Venu X1's Training Readiness score is genuinely useful for managing the combined load. The key insight from WIRED's breakdown: Training Status evaluates long-term trend; Training Readiness evaluates today. Check Readiness before a session, not just Status. A Productive Training Status combined with a low Readiness score (under 40) means you've been training well — but today is not the day to push. HRV Status trending "Unbalanced" over 5+ consecutive days is the clearest early-warning signal that total load is outpacing recovery.

Did You Know?

Garmin's HRV Status uses a 7-day rolling window rather than a single-night measurement. This smooths out the noise from one bad night's sleep (caused by a late meal, a drink, or poor sleep position) while still catching sustained trends. The practical implication: a single low HRV reading is not a signal to rest — five consecutive low readings is.

6 April 1453: The Cannons Open on Constantinople

55
Days the siege lasted

On April 5th, 1453, the Ottoman forces of Sultan Mehmed II had fully surrounded Constantinople's land walls while the fleet blockaded the Bosporus. No supply ships could enter. No defenders could escape. Mehmed sent an emissary demanding unconditional surrender — and received no response. The following day, April 6th, his cannons opened fire.

What made this siege historically unprecedented was not the scale of the army — though 60,000-80,000 Ottoman troops against 8,000 Byzantine defenders was overwhelming — but the artillery. Mehmed had commissioned a Hungarian gunsmith named Urban to build cannon powerful enough to destroy Constantinople's famous Theodosian Walls. Urban's largest gun, known as the Basilica, was 8 metres long, fired a 600kg stone cannonball, and required 60 oxen to transport. It had never been done before. The walls that had protected Constantinople for a thousand years — walls that had repelled Goths, Huns, Avars, Arabs, Bulgars, and Crusaders — crumbled under sustained bombardment.

The city held for 55 days. Emperor Constantine XI Palaeologus, knowing relief would not come, reportedly removed his imperial regalia and died fighting in the final assault on May 29th. When Mehmed entered the city, he reportedly rode to Hagia Sophia, dismounted, took a handful of earth and poured it over his turban in a gesture of humility before God. He renamed the city Istanbul and made it the capital of his empire. The fall of Constantinople (Wikipedia) is conventionally used to mark the end of the Middle Ages.

Why It Resonates Now

The siege of Constantinople demonstrates something about strategic innovation that never goes stale: entrenched defensive systems that have worked for centuries can be broken by a single new capability applied with sufficient will. Urban's cannon was the technological disruption. Mehmed's willingness to invest in it, and to use it relentlessly, was the strategic decision. The city's defenders had the walls. The attacker had better artillery. The walls lost.
