One Month of War: The Strait of Hormuz and the Fracture No One Planned

The conflict in its fourth week. US Marines have deployed to the Gulf region. — CNN / Reuters
Day 29
of the US–Israel military campaign against Iran — the most significant armed conflict involving American forces since 2003

Thirty days in, and the most honest thing anyone in Washington can say about the war with Iran is that nobody planned for it to last this long, cost this much, or involve this much damage to global trade. Reuters' comprehensive assessment this week put it plainly: only hard choices remain.

Key Players
  • Mojtaba Khamenei — Iran's new Supreme Leader, hardline, has threatened to block Hormuz indefinitely
  • Donald Trump — extended the pause on Iran energy strikes until April 6, signalling willingness to negotiate
  • Israel Katz — Israeli Defence Minister warned strikes will "move up a level" this week
  • Viktor Gyökeres — scored a hat-trick for Sweden. Nothing to do with Iran, but somebody has to have a good week.

The Strait of Hormuz, 25 miles wide at its narrowest, carries roughly 20% of global oil supply through a body of water Iran has been mining, threatening, and intermittently blockading since the conflict began in late February. Iran's military leadership — specifically, the IRGC — has been targeting shipping across the Gulf, and the new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei has publicly committed to maintaining the blockade for as long as strikes on Iranian soil continue. That is not a negotiating posture. That is a war aim.

Trump's response this week was characteristically sprawling. In Miami on Friday he described Iran as "begging to make a deal" while simultaneously calling the Strait of Hormuz the "Strait of Trump" — briefly, before correcting himself. He extended a pause on strikes against Iran's energy sector until April 6, signalling some residual diplomatic intent. But his Defence establishment was simultaneously weighing a surge of up to 10,000 additional troops into the region. Those two things don't fit comfortably together.

Europe is fractured — and not quietly so. France has explicitly said it will never participate in military operations to unblock Hormuz during active hostilities. Britain has approved US use of RAF Fairford and Diego Garcia for strikes on Iranian missile sites — a meaningful escalation — but stopped well short of direct naval participation in any Hormuz coalition. Japan cited constitutional constraints and said no. The pattern is consistent: allies support stabilisation in principle, but won't join the war itself. That gap between rhetoric and commitment is likely to define the next phase of the conflict more than any battlefield development.

The Angle

The most underreported story of the week isn't the strikes on Tehran — it's Ukraine exporting drone-defence expertise to five Gulf states. Kyiv has deployed hundreds of specialists to the UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, and Jordan to advise on interception of Iranian Shahed-type drones. Ukraine, which is fighting for its life, is simultaneously acting as a global military consultancy. That's an extraordinary thing to be happening. It's also a reminder that the war in Ukraine didn't end — it was just displaced from the front pages by an even bigger fire.

"We're closer than ever to the rise of the Middle East that is finally free at last from Iranian terror."

— Donald Trump, Miami, March 28, 2026

The Pakistan–Turkey–Egypt–Saudi quartet meeting in Islamabad on Sunday to discuss de-escalation is the most significant diplomatic development of the week. None of those countries want a wider regional war. All of them have economic exposure to Hormuz. Whether they have sufficient leverage over either Washington or Tehran is a different question — and the answer, on current evidence, is probably no.

    Italy–Northern Ireland: The Night Tonali Came Home

    Italy won 2–0 and it wasn't as comfortable as the scoreline suggests. Northern Ireland, set up in a compact 5-4-1 by their coaching staff, gave the Azzurri genuine problems through the first half. For long stretches it looked like Italy — a team under existential pressure to end a 12-year World Cup absence — might be heading for another catastrophe.

    Then Sandro Tonali happened. This was his night, and there's no point pretending otherwise. Tonali served an 18-month ban for illegal gambling that kept him out of international football until earlier this season. He has spent the intervening period training ferociously at Newcastle, visibly reconstructing himself. In Bergamo, with the pressure at its most acute, he scored and assisted Moise Kean's late clincher. The relief on his face at full time was something different from celebration — it was closer to release.

    The Take

    Northern Ireland shouldn't be ashamed of Thursday. They competed with a team four times their FIFA ranking for 75 minutes, played disciplined tactical football, and gave Italy serious problems at a ground the Azzurri were supposed to control. This was a good Northern Ireland team playing at its ceiling. Italy are just — barely — above it. Tuesday's final against Bosnia-Herzegovina will be uncomfortably tight.

    Run Stronger: The Science Is Simpler Than You Think — and More Specific Than You'd Hope

    Two significant pieces of strength-training research landed in March and they point in the same direction: the science of resistance training for runners is better understood than it's ever been, and the conclusions are both more encouraging and more specific than most generic fitness content acknowledges.

    First, the ACSM — the American College of Sports Medicine — published its first major update to resistance training guidelines in 17 years. The headline finding: even small amounts of resistance training improve strength, muscle size, and physical function. The key is consistency, not perfection. Strict adherence to the "ideal" training protocol is no longer supported by the evidence. Personal preference, enjoyment, and sustainability matter more.

    Second, a meta-analysis of 31 studies published in Runner's World has settled what kind of strength training actually improves running economy — and the answer depends heavily on what kind of runner you are:

    Strength Training for Runners — Method by Goal
    MethodBest ForMechanism
    Heavy loads, low reps (3-5 sets of 3-5)High-level runners, faster pacesNeuromuscular adaptations; improved rate of force development in short ground-contact time
    Plyometric trainingLower-speed endurance runnersIncreases tendon elongation; improves energy storage for longer efforts
    Combined methodsRecreational runners (most of us)Best of both — safer entry point, produces meaningful economy gains
    Sub-maximal load (light weight, high rep)Not recommended aloneInsufficient for economy changes; combine with heavy or plyo work
    Isometric trainingSpecific conditions onlyShort-period contractions simulating running biomechanics; limited standalone benefit

    The practical takeaway for a recreational runner with gym access: 2 sessions of resistance training per week, anchored around heavy compound lower-body work (Romanian deadlifts, Bulgarian split squats, trap bar deadlifts) plus plyometric work (box jumps, broad jumps, single-leg hops), is the evidence-backed combination. Sub-maximal, high-rep work alone — the "do some squats" advice — doesn't generate running economy improvements on its own.

    The Take

    The most interesting finding from the ACSM guidelines is actually the permission it gives. The old model — do this exact programme, this many sets, this many days — is gone. What the evidence actually supports is "do something, do it consistently, and choose methods you can maintain." That's not an excuse to phone it in; it's an invitation to build a training habit around what works in your life rather than an idealised prescription that fails after six weeks.

    Did You Know?

    Plyometric training was found to be the most effective strength method for slower-speed, lower-level runners — a genuinely surprising result, since most beginners are steered away from explosive training. The proposed mechanism is tendon elongation and energy storage improvement, which disproportionately benefits those running at lower intensities for longer periods.

    Easter Sunday, 845 AD: When Ragnar Sailed 120 Ships Up the Seine and Took Paris for Silver

    On this day in 845 AD — Easter Sunday — a fleet of 120 Viking longships carrying more than 5,000 warriors reached the city of Paris. They entered. They plundered it. And then, when Frankish King Charles the Bald offered them 2,570 kilograms of gold and silver to leave, they left.

    The Norse chieftain leading the raid was known as Reginherus to Frankish chroniclers — and has been tentatively identified, by later tradition, as the legendary Ragnar Lodbrok. Whether or not that identification is accurate, the feat was extraordinary: a fleet sailed up the Seine from the coast, defeated a Frankish army division that was foolishly split between both banks of the river, and arrived at the most important city in Western Europe on its most symbolically significant day.

    Charles the Bald, grandson of Charlemagne, faced a problem with no clean solution. His army had been defeated — partly through a tactical blunder of his own making, dividing his forces on either bank of the Seine when the Vikings could attack each half separately. With no relief army available, he paid. Seven thousand French livres in gold and silver — roughly 2,570 kilograms — in what became the first of thirteen such ransom payments the Franks made to Viking raiders. The French term for it, danegeld, became the word for tribute-under-duress across medieval Europe.

    The Angle

    The choice of Easter Sunday was almost certainly deliberate. Arriving on the holiest day in the Christian calendar, when the city would be most crowded and the symbolic humiliation most acute, was a Viking statement of intent. Charles the Bald paying the ransom rather than fighting was also a statement — of political realism, if not military courage. The precedent it set, however, was catastrophic: Paris would be attacked repeatedly across the next four decades, culminating in the far more famous siege of 885-886. The lesson "pay the Danes and they'll go away" was correct in the short term and wrong across the century.

    The episode sits in an interesting historical moment: it precedes the settlement of Normandy (911 AD) by only 66 years. The Northmen who sacked Paris in 845 were the forebears — culturally and often literally — of the Normans who would transform France and then England. The Vikings didn't just raid Europe. They became it.
