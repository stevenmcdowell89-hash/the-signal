Both places are five-star camping villages with chlorine, gelato, lifeguards and a kids' club. That is roughly where the similarity ends. One sits on a Catalan beach, hugging Europe's biggest theme-park resort; the other slopes down to the largest lake in Italy with Verona an hour away. Nine substantive rounds, no prices, no flat winner — this is what the source pool actually says.

Five-star against five-star — the numbers laid out before any of the prose has to take a position.

Source spine

The base figures here are official-site facilities lists cross-checked against three independent operator pages and the two TripAdvisor master records. Reviewer scores updated 28 April 2026.

Sanguli runs ahead on review volume — almost three times as many TripAdvisor reviews, and a higher average — but the two scores are within a fifth of a star, well inside noise. The structural gap is in the water complex: Piani's seven pools and twenty combined slides outscale Sanguli on paper, while Sanguli's five pools are themed in a way Piani's are not.

Piani's geographic advantage is the lake: direct access, with the kind of cool, swimmable water that does not blink in 31°C heat. Sanguli's geographic advantage is gravitational: PortAventura's roller-coasters are visible from the resort, the Catalan coast is one road away, and Tarragona's Roman amphitheatre is twenty minutes by car.

II What Each Place Is Round II — orientation
II
II Orientation · 23–30 August 2026 Two villages

Mediterranean mega-camping, Italian lakeside village

Sanguli Salou sits on the Costa Daurada, a stretch of Catalan coast about ninety minutes south-west of Barcelona. The gates open straight onto the Platja de Llevant, a long Blue Flag beach with the kind of soft, gradually-deepening sand that sells postcards. The site itself, mapped on the official plan, runs from an "African" entrance at the top — guarded by giraffe figures on the way in — down through five themed zones to a beach gate at the bottom. A free Trenet mini-train shuttles guests across the thirty hectares. The whole resort is, in a phrase used by both TripAdvisor reviewers and a YouTube vlogger walking the entire site, "huge yet immaculate".

It is also a resort with neighbours. PortAventura World — Europe's third-busiest theme park, plus the Ferrari Land annex — is six kilometres up the coast, an hourly €1 Empresa Plana bus from a stop within walking distance of Sanguli's reception. Salou itself, a bustle of restaurants and seaside bars, is a "twenty-minute walk" from the gates. Tarragona, with its Roman amphitheatre and forum, is twenty minutes by car. Reus airport is nine kilometres away.

Piani di Clodia is the opposite proposition geographically. It is inland in the sense that no ocean is within reach — but it is on the very edge of Lake Garda, the largest lake in Italy, and the lake itself functions as the resort's beach. The site terraces down a slope on the eastern shore, just outside the medieval town of Lazise; Life With Tiny Humans describes the walk to town as "around 20–30 minutes… looking out onto Lake Garda. The walk is around 20–30 minutes depending on how tired/enthusiastic your small people are. The views along the lake are gorgeous and it's flat the whole way." Lazise's medieval walls and lakefront harbour sit at the end of that walk; Verona's Roman arena is forty minutes by car.

The cultural register, accordingly, differs. Sanguli is a Mediterranean theme-park-adjacent mega-camping with a neon-lit town in walking distance — a holiday whose rhythm is built around the beach, the pools, the amphitheatre stage and a day at PortAventura. Piani di Clodia is a five-star Italian camping village whose rhythm is built around the lake, the pools, a ferry to Sirmione, an evening shuttle into a walled town, and a day-trip to Verona or Gardaland. Both have animation teams, kids' clubs, sports courts and supermarkets. Neither has a wave pool. The choice is not between two camping holidays — it is between two countries' versions of what a five-star camping holiday is for.

III Accommodation Round III — what each premium tier looks like
III

Two operating models

Themed zones vs a single sloped village

Sanguli's accommodation is sorted into four named themed zones, mapped against the pool areas: Africa (Samburu, Nuba, Masai lodges and Safari Tents at the top), Caribbean (Villas), Polynesia (Bungalows) and Mediterranean (Mobile Homes). The branding is more than skin-deep: a Mini Travellers review of a Samburu Lodge — three bedrooms, two bathrooms, sleeps seven — notes that "what we really loved about the lodge though was the outside space and the positioning. We've seen sites where the lodges/caravans etc are almost touching and this wasn't the case." A resort tour by TheDandylion5, who stayed in a Tahiti Suite, calls the resort as a whole "amazing" and notes the theming follows you across the property: "it does change theming, it changes style as you're going through, so you can tell."

Piani di Clodia's accommodation is one village rather than four — bungalows clustered around a central square (Catullo and S3 categories) plus, at the lakeside premium tier, the Maxicaravan Lake series with private hot tubs and slope-terraced lake views. Life With Tiny Humans, writing in June 2025 about a Maxicaravan Crystal Lake stay, describes "a private hot tub overlooking the lake, which we used every night without fail. The sunsets, the stars, the tired-but-happy feet soaking away — absolute bliss. There is lots of lush grass and flowers/plants around this accommodation, giving you plenty of privacy." The unit had a hob, microwave, fridge with freezer box, and, importantly for late August, "air conditioning meaning the space was nice and cool as it got up to 30°C outside some days." Beyond the maxicaravans there is the Borgo dei Colli eco range and a separate Relais Villa Clodia hotel of ten suites with its own spa.

Quality is consistent at both sites and dips occasionally on the same axis: outdoor space and finish good, indoor build-quality variable. A 2024 Piani reviewer reports a pitch "good size and felt private enough", though staff accommodation opposite kept them up. A 2025 Sanguli reviewer praises "everything top class" alongside a 2018 note that "wifi could be better… one of the pools could be heated." Neither resort has a five-star reputation that collapses under scrutiny.

A reviewer's checklist

The five things repeat across Piani's reviewer pool: hot tub on the lake, AC indoors, lush grass between units, separate kids' pool, walk to Lazise. Sanguli's repeat list is different — Africa zone first, lifeguard density, amphitheatre nightly, Eurocamp pitches, walkable Salou.

Round III verdict

Contextual draw — themed-zone variety vs a single premium tier with the better view

The reviewer pool gives Sanguli the win on accommodation variety: four named themed zones, Tahiti Suites, lodges built around the African-savannah idea, and outdoor space that survives a peak-summer occupation. It gives Piani the win on the premium-tier ceiling: a Maxicaravan Crystal Lake with a private hot tub on the lake is a unit Sanguli does not run an equivalent of. Holiday party priorities decide this round — a family that wants to stay in the African theme picks Sanguli; a couple-plus-kids that wants a hot tub on Italy's largest lake picks Piani.

IV Pools, Slides, Water Round IV — the deep round
IV
IV Water complex · capacity · theming Five vs seven

Five themed pools or seven pools and a lazy river

This is the round that holiday-park decisions most often pivot on, and the round where the comparison is most worth doing carefully. Both resorts have water complexes that reviewers describe as a primary reason to book; they reach that praise by very different routes.

Sanguli — five themed pools, theming as the headline

Sanguli's water offering is structured as five named pool areas spread across the property, each with its own bar, sun-bed run and lifeguard team. The TheDandylion5 resort tour walks all five: the Africa pool at the top of the site, then Guli, then Fiesta with the swim-up Bar Oasis, then Happy with two slides, then Relax at the bottom near the beach gate. The vlogger flags that "this is the Africa pool — there's two tube slides over there, two little slides here, and this one is slightly heated. The rest can be a little bit cold on a cold cloudy day." A family review at Who's the Mummy? describes "a tropical pool with waterfall and children's area, an Olympic sized pool with two water slides, and another two pools with slides and children's area." The same reviewer adds the most reassuring single sentence written about either resort's water facilities: "There were more lifeguards on duty at the swimming pools than I've ever seen at this sort of resort before."

The theming on the Africa pool, in particular, is the line in the sand. A separate Sanguli Africa pool video describes the area: "two pools, multiple big slides, toddler slides and a cool africa themed area with cheetahs, elephants, vulture, hippos and at the back side of the pool even a giraffe." TheDandylion5 echoes the point: "this is quite a nicely themed pool because it's got all the elephants in the water… it doesn't look cheap, it doesn't feel cheap, it feels like they spent a [lot]." A 2018 TripAdvisor reviewer calls the resort overall "huge yet immaculate with beautiful landscaping and flowers. The pools were wonderful, well supervised and each with their own bar."

The complaints converge on two things: temperature and crowding. "One of the pools could be heated as while we were there it was a little bit colder than last year and kids only lasted approx 15 mins – 20 mins in the pools," writes a long-running returner. A June 2025 reviewer (TedC) goes further: "Pools close between 2 and 3, I believe this is to have a reset and tidy up however on two occasions when we came at 3pm there was poo floating around in the pool/on the pool side." And the per-pool capacity warning is documented across multiple seasons: "Numerous pools which kids loved, slides great for their ages, but by midday [crowded]".

The case for Sanguli's water complex is that the pools are spaced widely enough that, in Who's the Mummy's framing, "the individual pools don't feel too crowded." The case against: none of the five is a true headline aquapark — for that, sources point users at Aquopolis, the day-ticket water park nearby.

Piani di Clodia — seven pools, a lazy river, lane swimming

Piani's water complex is structurally bigger — and reviewers consistently flag it as the resort's strongest single asset. Allcamps documents "seven swimming pools and you can choose between eight different water slides to enjoy a full day swimming. The little ones have a separate paddling pool" — the eight figures-of-merit are the adrenaline slides; the additional twelve slides for younger children sit on the kids' pool. A pool feature page on TripAdvisor singles out "the lazy river is a really nice touch", and a separate Dutch reviewer on the same operator's official site writes that "swimming pools are fantastic, from young to old. A big plus of the swimming pool is that there is a section for swimming lanes. Good supervision of swimming pools." That lane section is unusual at a five-star camping village and is one of the few first-page differentiators a reviewer can name without being prompted.

Heating is an asymmetry too. Life With Tiny Humans records that "the main kids' pool is heated" — it got busy on a public-holiday weekend but "much quieter midweek". A 2024 reviewer adds the adult pools "were quite cold" — a structural mirror of the Sanguli pattern.

The capacity story is, on reviewer accounts, materially better at Piani than at Sanguli during peak. The clearest single signal in the source pool comes from a July 2022 reviewer: "This is a big campsite that even during this peak period managed to make things feel not over crowded, ie always managed to get 5 sun loungers at the pool no matter what time!" Piani's VacanceSelect score for the pool category is 9.6 across 97 reviews, the single highest sub-score on the platform — well above the operator's 8.5 animation rating and 8 bars-and-restaurants rating.

The against-case for Piani's water complex sits, perhaps surprisingly, at the bar that serves it: a 2024 reviewer describes the bar process as "very weird as you have to queue up to order and get a ticket and then queue up in a separate line to actually get your drinks." It is the only consistent operational complaint that surfaces across multiple Piani reviews.

Where the round actually splits

On volume, Piani wins clearly: seven pools to five, twenty combined slides to a smaller (but unpublished) Sanguli total, plus a lazy river and a lane section that Sanguli does not match. On theming, Sanguli wins clearly: the Africa pool's elephant and giraffe figures are a genuinely distinctive piece of resort design, with the four other named pools providing a coherent backdrop to the four named accommodation zones. On peak-week capacity, the reviewer signal favours Piani — the "always got 5 sun loungers" line and the 9.6 pool sub-score are stronger than anything in the Sanguli pool reviewer pool, which contains repeated mid-afternoon crowding complaints. On lifeguard density and pool cleanliness, Sanguli wins on density (Who's the Mummy's "more lifeguards… than I've ever seen") but takes a hit on the 2025 cleanliness incident; Piani has no equivalent flag.

"There were more lifeguards on duty at the swimming pools than I've ever seen at this sort of resort before."

— Who's the Mummy?, on Sanguli

Round IV verdict

Piani edges it on volume and capacity; Sanguli's theming is the only round-stealing variable

If the priority on this round is sheer water-complex throughput — pools, slides, lazy rivers, lane swimming, a lower probability of fighting for a sunbed in the second week of August — the source pool consistently points to Piani di Clodia. If the priority is themed pools that look like a destination in their own right, with denser lifeguard coverage, Sanguli's Africa pool and the four pools beside it pull this one. Eight or so reviewer voices feed each side; the convergence is on Piani for capacity, Sanguli for theming.

V Food Round V — on-site dining
V

On-site kitchens, supermarkets, and what's over the wall

The same menu — or a separate kitchen with a queue

Sanguli runs more on-site dining than Piani in absolute terms. Reviewers and the resort's own listings consistently name a Victoria restaurant in the Africa zone, a Tarraco restaurant attached to a deli counter, the main Sangulí restaurant overlooking the central pool, a pizza-and-takeaway bar that runs to 11 pm, and supermarkets within the resort. The Victoria restaurant in particular is a piece of theming in its own right: TheDandylion5 filmed it on her tour: "this is the Victoria restaurant. This is the one that has the robots that bring the food to your table." A TripAdvisor reviewer writes that "the main restaurant/bar is gorgeous and overlooks the pool. You could quite easily believe you were in a much more expensive venue."

The recurring caveat across multiple Sanguli reviewers — bloggers, TripAdvisor and YouTube vloggers alike — is menu repetition. Different restaurants on the same site share, in reviewers' descriptions, a substantially similar menu, which makes a week's worth of evenings on-site read more like one menu than four. A 2022 TripAdvisor reviewer puts it bluntly: "We did however find the restaurant situation was not great. Food over priced and presentation not great. We felt we were paying high prices for only average food." The fix that recurs across Sanguli reviews is to walk to the off-site supermarkets — a Mercadona five minutes away and a Michelangelo "across the road" — and self-cater more nights than originally planned, then walk into Salou for the variety. The pattern is "on-site restaurants for a couple of nights; everything else over the wall."

Walk into Lazise

Free shuttle 6–10 pm Mon–Fri; the lakeside path is flat the whole way, 20–30 minutes door-to-harbour. TripAdvisor counts more than 1,600 restaurants within ten miles of the gates.

Piani di Clodia runs fewer on-site restaurants — two, plus the pizzeria and the deli counter and a couple of gelato spots — but the source pool consistently flags one of them as the resort's culinary high point. An August 2024 TripAdvisor review writes: "If you are going to eat on site, I can highly recommend the Marc'Aurelio restaurant — this is a little oasis of calm and fantastic service (Probably because it is independent) and not to mention fantastic food. Go here, you will not be disappointed. Take out Pizza is also great near the main bar." The same reviewer, however, cuts the resort's other on-site restaurant deeply: "The main restaurant in the square Catullo left a lot to be desired. The food was just ok but the service was shocking. No one came to serve us until we asked about 4 people…"

A second TripAdvisor reviewer the same year describes the same split: "The other restaurant Marc'Aurelio was a much nicer experience. The staff were older and much nicer. The food was so much better there too." The signal across reviewers is unusually convergent: reviewers like Marc'Aurelio specifically because it operates independently of the campsite's catering team, and they avoid Catullo on busy weeks. The official site confirms Marc'Aurelio's character in a Piani's own restaurant page: "Ristorante-pizzeria Gourmet Marc'Aurelio, per gustare specialità e proposte stagionali. Scelte per adulti e bambini. Opzioni senza glutine."

"A little oasis of calm and fantastic service. Probably because it is independent — and not to mention fantastic food."

— TripAdvisor, August 2024 — on Piani's Marc'Aurelio

The differentiator that makes Piani's food round stronger than its on-site restaurant count suggests is what is over the wall. Life With Tiny Humans singles out Classique and Cozzeria Mussels and More in Lazise; the free shuttle runs 6–10 pm Monday to Friday, and the lakeside walk in is "flat the whole way" — a 20–30 minute traverse with views. TripAdvisor lists more than 1,600 restaurants within ten miles of the site. Lazise is a working medieval town, not a holiday-park dormitory; Sanguli's equivalent walk into Salou opens onto a Mediterranean resort strip rather than a centro storico — a different texture.

A subtler differentiator: Piani's on-site supermarket is praised by Life With Tiny Humans as "fantastic — way cheaper than the shops we popped into in nearby Lazise", with a deli closed 12–4, bakery open until 10 am, and gelato at €2 a scoop. Sanguli's supermarkets are functional but reviewers consistently push guests off-site for grocery savings. Both reward self-caterers — Piani's bakery-deli-supermarket triangle is the more interesting on-site setup.

Round V verdict

Piani on quality and adjacency; Sanguli on on-site novelty (and only there)

The source pool gives Piani the food round on two converging lines: an independent on-site restaurant (Marc'Aurelio) that reviewers single out repeatedly as a high point, and a 20-minute walk that ends in a working medieval town with hundreds of independent kitchens. Sanguli wins the on-site novelty line — the Victoria's robot-waiter theming, the central restaurant's "much more expensive venue" finish — but loses the on-site quality line because of the menu-repetition caveat that recurs across reviewer types. If the priority on the food round is variety per pound spent across a week, this round goes to Piani; if the priority is theming and a takeaway pizza window open until midnight, Sanguli takes it back.

Sources: TripAdvisor, official Piani restaurant page, Life With Tiny Humans, TheDandylion5, TripAdvisor (Sanguli), TripAdvisor (Sanguli, food).

VI Beyond the Pools Round VI — entertainment, sport, animation
VI

Amphitheatre nights versus a ferry ticket

Sanguli's strongest non-pool asset, on the reviewer pool's reading, is its open-air amphitheatre and the show-booking team that programmes it. A TripAdvisor reviewer describes it in a way that captures the scale: "There is a large amphitheatre type stage that hosts discos and live shows. We were lucky enough to see The Lion King performance which was completely free, but could easily charge 15-20 euros for if it was at a theatre. As it was Halloween, the staff went to great lengths to decorate the camp site, and they set up a 'passage de terror' in one of the toilet blocks." A different reviewer logs the rhythm: "Entertainment in the evening at the amphitheatre is worth a visit. Get there early enough (around 9pm) to grab a table and you get waiter service for drinks." TheDandylion5 observed in her resort tour that the amphitheatre runs "until around midnight maybe a little bit before."

The point is not just the shows — the amphitheatre draws the whole site in for two hours every night. Who's the Mummy also flags: "The two reps for the children's club were particularly brilliant, and Flea loved the weekly 'Wet and Wild' session which is basically a huge, chaotic water battle." Beyond evening shows: a Trenet mini-train, sports courts, a "no noise after midnight" rule reviewers report is "well enforced", and a visible security team on patrol.

Piani's beyond-pool offering is broader on paper but lower on praise. The site has tennis and padel courts, an astro football pitch, a gym, a kids' club, a mini-disco, and organised football sessions. The lake adds a layer Sanguli does not have: pedalo and SUP hire, an hour's hire of a no-licence boat (Life With Tiny Humans: "€60 well spent"), and a ferry from Lazise to Peschiera del Garda. The off-site theme parks — Caneva Aquapark, Movieland, Gardaland — sit within fifteen minutes' drive. A 2018 reviewer writes: "There is yoga, outdoor ping pong, BBQs, cycle hire and so much more… adults liked the path at bottom of the park leading straight onto Lake Garda."

VacanceSelect breakdown

9.6 swimming pool · 9.2 sports facilities · 9.6 surroundings · 8.5 animation · 8.0 bars and restaurants. The pool-and-surroundings axis is where the resort accumulates its score.

The complaint that recurs across Piani's reviewer pool is the evening entertainment. An Allcamps reviewer (Anita Donaldson, June 2023) rates the resort highly overall but is direct on the nights: "Size of campsite excellent. Pools great. Night time entertainment poor." A separate TripAdvisor reviewer says outright: "Pianni di Clodia is great if you just want to base yourself there… If you are looking for a relaxing family holiday unfortunately, I cannot recommend this holiday park." Life With Tiny Humans walks past the evening shows "on the way home from Lazise" — an atmosphere they observed but didn't engage with.

VacanceSelect's review-category breakdown is the cleanest summary of the asymmetry: a 9.6 score for Piani's swimming pool, a 9.2 for sports facilities, a 9.6 for surroundings — but only an 8.5 for animation and an 8 for bars and restaurants. Piani is, in reviewer language, a base camp; Sanguli is a destination.

— Anita Donaldson, Allcamps reviewer, Piani, June 2023

Sanguli — animation

Amphitheatre nightly; kids' disco from 7pm; Lion King free shows. Reviewer convergence: evenings are the strongest beyond-pool axis.

Piani — sports & animation

VacanceSelect 9.2 sports / 8.5 animation. Tennis, padel, gym, astro pitch, boat hire. "Pools great, night time entertainment poor" recurs across reviews.

Round VI verdict

Sanguli takes the evening; Piani takes the daytime activity range

Sanguli wins the round if the metric is whether the resort fills the evening hours by itself — the amphitheatre's nightly programme is, on multiple reviewers' accounts, the resort's strongest single piece of beyond-pool infrastructure. Piani wins the round if the metric is daytime activity range, especially water-sports, sports facilities, and proximity to off-site attractions; the lake adds boat-hire and SUP options Sanguli has no equivalent for. Where the round splits is whether reviewers expect the campsite or the surroundings to entertain them after dark — Sanguli is built around the assumption it does; Piani is built around the assumption Lazise will.

A themed-resort identity at one site versus a lake-view identity at the other — and which one wears better through a peak-season week.

VII Theming & Atmosphere Round VII — what each place looks like
VII

Africa, Caribbean, Polynesia — or a hillside above a lake

Sanguli's theming is intentional, named, and reaches into every layer of the resort. The four accommodation zones (Africa, Caribbean, Polynesia, Mediterranean) have matching pool areas, signage, planting and even restaurant decor. The Africa zone — the entrance to the resort — is the most heavily themed: the giraffes outside reception, the cheetah and elephant figures around the pool, the Samburu, Nuba and Masai lodge ranges, and the Africa-named pizza bar. As TheDandylion5's vlog repeatedly observes during her walk-through: "it does change theming, it changes style as you're going through, so you can tell." A TripAdvisor reviewer describes the resort as "huge yet immaculate with beautiful landscaping and flowers" — the landscaping is doing as much work as the figures.

Piani di Clodia, by contrast, has minimal explicit theming. There is no Africa zone, no Caribbean villas, no Polynesia bungalow strip — there is a slope-terraced Italian holiday park whose visual identity is what is over the wall. The "theming", in reviewer language, is the lake itself. A Dutch reviewer writes: "Definitely one of the best campings at Lake Garda, with almost everywhere a beautiful view of the lake or mountains." A separate Dutch review on the same site adds: "Big campsite like a small town but nice and quiet." The resort gets its character from its setting and its proximity to a working medieval town, rather than from internal world-building.

An honest line converges across Piani's reviewer pool: the resort sometimes feels less Italian than its setting promises — the bar's queue-and-ticket system, the restaurant waits, the framing as "great if you just want to base yourself there… visiting other places like Lazise or Verona." The atmosphere reviewers praise is the lakeside walk, the harbour at Lazise, the terraced bungalows looking down to water. The resort is a lens onto Lake Garda; it does not impose an identity on top of it.

Two strategies, both five-star

Sanguli builds an identity inside its walls. Piani borrows one from outside them. Both approaches collect five-star reviewer pools; the choice is taste, not quality.

The themed-resort case and the lakeside case do not compete on the same axis. Sanguli's Africa giraffes and Polynesia tiki posts are choices about what a campsite should feel like. Piani's terraced bungalows and harbour walks are choices about what a campsite should not get in the way of. The asymmetry is the point.

"Beautiful view of the lake or mountains." — Dutch Piani reviewer · "Huge yet immaculate." — TripAdvisor Sanguli

A subtler atmospheric difference shows up in evening reviews. Sanguli's after-dark feel is bound up with the amphitheatre — the resort feels populated. Piani's evening centre of gravity is the shuttle to Lazise. A reviewer's preferred evening — the Lion King in an amphitheatre or a long lakeside walk into a walled town — is the variable that decides this round.

Round VII verdict

Sanguli on internal theming; Piani on the borrowed atmosphere of its setting

If theming is a category — the deliberate world-building of a resort that decides its own identity — Sanguli wins this round clearly. The Africa zone is a piece of resort design with intent, and the four-zone structure is one of the most coherent pieces of identity in the European camping market. If atmosphere is the category — what the resort feels like once the guest is inside it — the round splits along reviewer taste. Sanguli feels like a Mediterranean themed-resort village with a beach at the end. Piani feels like a hillside village above a lake, with a walled medieval town at the end of the path. Both are five-star ways of building a holiday. They are not competing on the same axis.

Sources: TheDandylion5, TripAdvisor (Sanguli), Piani Dutch site, TripAdvisor (Piani).

PortAventura at the wall and Tarragona twenty minutes away versus Sirmione, Verona and three theme parks within fifteen kilometres.

VIII Location Round VIII — what's outside
VIII
VIII Surroundings · access · day-trip range PortAventura · Verona

Two countries, two day-trip economies

Sanguli's surrounding-attractions case is one of the strongest in European camping. PortAventura World — the third-most-visited theme park in Europe — is six kilometres up the coast, with an Empresa Plana bus running hourly from a stop a short walk from the resort gates at €1 a ride. Ferrari Land sits inside the same complex. Aquopolis, the local outdoor water park, is twenty minutes away and gets repeated mentions in the Sanguli reviewer pool as the day-trip that fills a wet afternoon. Tarragona, with its UNESCO Roman amphitheatre, forum and circus, is twenty minutes by car. Reus airport is nine kilometres from the gates, and the resort runs an airport transfer service from arrivals. Salou itself, a Mediterranean coastal-resort town with a long beachfront, is in walking distance — a "twenty-minute walk" per multiple TripAdvisor reviewers.

Piani's surrounding-attractions case is built differently — a denser cluster of lower-cost day options rather than one marquee park. Allcamps documents the headline distances: Caneva Aquapark 4.6 kilometres (nine minutes' drive), Movieland 5.3 kilometres (six minutes' drive), Gardaland — the Disney-of-Italy theme park — 8.8 kilometres (eleven minutes' drive). Lazise's medieval walls are 0.4 kilometres away. The Lazise–Peschiera del Garda ferry — €39 return for a family of five, per Life With Tiny Humans — runs from the harbour and connects to Sirmione, the spa-town peninsula on the southern shore. Verona's Roman arena and Romeo-and-Juliet old town are roughly forty minutes by car. Verona airport is 18 minutes' drive; the resort, per Life With Tiny Humans, runs a free transfer if guests email ahead.

The structural difference is gravity. Sanguli has one massive day-trip — PortAventura — that reshapes the holiday: most families book Sanguli for the PortAventura adjacency. Piani has no single equivalent of that gravity. Gardaland is closer in absolute terms than PortAventura is to Sanguli, but it is one of three nearby theme parks rather than a single dominant one, and the lake itself competes for any given day.

Transport access is at parity — both resorts 9–18 minutes from a low-cost European airport, both with free or near-free shuttles into their nearest town, both with flat lakeside or beachfront paths into walking-distance towns. Piani is structurally weaker on rail (train station eight kilometres away). Sanguli is weaker on off-week range: once PortAventura has been done twice, alternative day-trips are Tarragona and Barcelona (ninety minutes), versus Piani's denser local cluster.

Round VIII verdict

Sanguli for one marquee day-trip; Piani for a denser day-trip cluster

If the priority is a single, world-class theme-park day right next door, Sanguli's PortAventura adjacency is a genuine differentiator and one of the strongest specific cases for booking the resort at all. If the priority is variety — three theme parks within fifteen kilometres, a working medieval town within four hundred metres, a Roman city forty minutes away, and a working ferry on the lake — Piani's location stack is broader. The round splits on whether the holiday reads as a theme-park trip or a lake-and-day-trip trip.

Sources: TripAdvisor (Sanguli), Allcamps, Life With Tiny Humans.

The last week of European school holidays — peak heat, peak crowds — is where the two resorts diverge in a way the rest of the year does not show.

IX Late August Round IX — edge case
IX

Where peak-week heat lands hardest

Both resorts run at peak in the last week of August — it is the closing window of the European school holidays, when Catalan, Italian, German, French and British school calendars overlap. The reviewer pool flags it directly. A July 2022 Piani reviewer uses peak season as the framing of the strongest single line in the entire source pool: "even during this peak period managed to make things feel not over crowded, ie always managed to get 5 sun loungers at the pool no matter what time." Sanguli's peak-week reviewer signal goes the other way: "by midday [the pools were crowded]" recurs across multiple seasons, and the 2025 cleanliness incident logged in TedC's review happens at peak.

Peak-week temperature

Costa Daurada averages 31°C in the last week of August, with land breezes off the Mediterranean. Lake Garda averages 28–31°C in the same week, with 22°C lake water as escape valve.

Climate is the second axis. Costa Daurada averages 30–33°C across the last week of August, with land breezes off the Mediterranean providing some afternoon relief — but Sanguli's main pool block is not heavily shaded, and the Africa pool's only-slightly-heated water is the closest thing the resort has to a peak-week heat refuge. The beach 150 metres away is the practical late-afternoon move. Lake Garda averages 28–31°C in the same week, with the lake itself functioning as a climate stabiliser — daytime swims in 22°C lake water are a fundamentally different proposition from afternoon swims in mid-thirties pool water. Piani's pool complex is also more shaded than Sanguli's, on the available aerial photography.

A third asymmetry is the size-and-spread effect. Both resorts are within a few percentage points of each other on pitch count and footprint, but Piani's seven pools are spread over a sloping site that funnels guests across multiple terraces, and reviewers note the lake walk as a low-friction way out of the resort at peak. Sanguli's five pools are spaced across a larger footprint but the resort's vertical structure is flat, and the off-site escape valves — Salou's beachfront, Tarragona, PortAventura — are themselves at peak in the same week.

"Pools close between 2 and 3, I believe this is to have a reset and tidy up however on two occasions when we came at 3pm there was poo floating around in the pool/on the pool side."

— Sanguli TripAdvisor reviewer (TedC), June 2025

"Even during this peak period managed to make things feel not over crowded — ie always managed to get 5 sun loungers at the pool no matter what time."

— TripAdvisor reviewer, Piani di Clodia, peak season July 2022

Round IX verdict

Piani's peak-week signal is materially better than Sanguli's

This is one of the few rounds where the source pool consistently leans one way. Reviewer voices across multiple peak-summer seasons describe Piani as absorbing peak-week capacity well — the "5 sun loungers at any time" line is a single quote, but the supporting signal from the broader pool (9.6 pool sub-score on VacanceSelect, large-footprint reviewer language, the lake as escape valve) corroborates it. Sanguli's peak-week reviewer pool documents the opposite pattern: midday pool crowding, the mid-afternoon close-and-reset that only sometimes lands cleanly, and a shaded-pool deficit in 30°C+ heat. If a holiday week is going to fall in the absolute peak of European school holidays, the source pool gives Piani a structural advantage on this round.

Sources: TripAdvisor (Piani peak), TripAdvisor (Sanguli), VacanceSelect.

Across nine rounds, the source pool consistently refuses to award a flat winner. Contextual / draw

If the priorities are theming, beach, theme-park gravity, evening entertainment

— it is Sanguli Salou. The Africa-Caribbean-Polynesia-Mediterranean four-zone structure is one of the most coherent pieces of theming in European camping; the 150-metre walk to a Blue Flag beach is a hard-to-beat structural advantage; PortAventura's gravity reshapes the holiday around it; and the amphitheatre's "Lion King" nights and disco programming make the resort a self-contained evening venue in a way Piani is not. If the holiday is built around theme-park days, beachfront afternoons and amphitheatre evenings, the source pool consistently points one way.

If the priorities are pool variety, lake bathing, day-trip culture, and on-site dining quality

— it is Piani di Clodia. Seven pools, eight adrenaline slides plus twelve baby slides, a lazy river and a lane-swim section give the resort the strongest single water complex of the two on volume; Marc'Aurelio's independent kitchen is the round-winning on-site dining call; the 20–30-minute lakeside walk into Lazise opens a thousand-plus restaurants and a working medieval town; the ferry to Sirmione and the eighteen-minute drive to Verona open a day-trip economy Sanguli's surroundings simply do not match. And on peak-week capacity — the August week in question — Piani's reviewer signal is materially stronger.

The third frame

The deciding question is not which resort is "better" — both are genuine five-star camping villages with strong reviewer pools — but what kind of holiday is being booked. A Costa Daurada theme-park-and-beach week is one kind of holiday; a Lake Garda lakeside-and-day-trip week is another. Booked for the right reasons, both resorts deliver on their own terms; booked against their own structure — Sanguli for a quiet lakeside reading week, Piani for amphitheatre nights — both will read as compromised.
