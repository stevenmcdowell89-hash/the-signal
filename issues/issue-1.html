The World This Week

The War That Wasn't Supposed to Last This Long

Iran, Week Four: The Strait Is Still Closed, and Nobody Has a Real Exit

The US–Israel military campaign against Iran entered its fourth week on March 21 with no clear endgame in sight — and the costs are no longer just geopolitical. Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly 20% of global oil and LNG ordinarily flows, is now the dominant economic event in the world. Brent crude touched $119 a barrel during the week before settling around $110–111 by Friday close — a roughly 50% surge since hostilities began at the end of February.

The US military claims it has "struck over 8,000 military targets" under Operation Epic Fury since the conflict began. The Pentagon says Iranian missile and drone attacks have fallen 90% since week one. But Iran's strategy has evolved: rather than fighting force-on-force, Tehran has shifted to targeting the region's energy infrastructure — striking oil facilities in Kuwait, the UAE, and most consequentially Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG hub. QatarEnergy declared force majeure on LNG exports after strikes on March 18 damaged infrastructure that analysts say could take up to five years to fully repair. Qatar accounts for roughly 20% of global LNG supply.

On Friday March 20, Trump signalled he was considering "winding down" US operations, prompting brief optimism in markets before oil spiked again. IEA chief Fatih Birol warned it could take up to six months to restore normal oil and gas flows. More than 30 countries have released emergency oil reserves — the largest such release in history, with the US contributing 172 million barrels from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve.

Civilian casualties on the Iranian side are severe. The death toll exceeds 2,300 civilians, including more than 200 children, with over a million displaced across the region. Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was confirmed killed in a US–Israel strike in Tehran; Iran declared 40 days of mourning.

The Strait of Hormuz closure is only secondarily a military story. It is primarily an energy market architecture story — and one that exposes how asymmetric the pain is. The US, now a major oil exporter, is insulated. Europe and Asia are not. Roughly 84% of Hormuz-transiting crude goes to Asia: Dubai crude has blown past $160. China has banned diesel and gasoline exports to hoard supply. South Korea imposed fuel price caps for the first time in 30 years. Japan is releasing 80 million barrels of strategic reserves. This war, even if it ends next week, has already stress-tested the entire architecture of global energy security in ways that will shape policy decisions — on renewables, on LNG infrastructure, on strategic reserves — for the next decade.

Central Banks Blink — But Won't Move

Fed, BoE, ECB, BoJ All Hold Rates as Energy Shock Scrambles the Calculus

The week of March 18 saw all four major central banks hold their benchmark rates unchanged — a decision that sounds simple until you understand the bind they're in. The Federal Reserve kept its rate at 3.5%–3.75%, its second consecutive pause of 2026. Chair Powell's press conference sent the Dow Jones down 769 points as he characterised the inflation picture as "frustrating."

The Bank of England voted unanimously to hold at 3.75%, but the minutes reveal an MPC acutely aware of a deteriorating picture. BoE staff now estimate UK CPI will be between 3% and 3.5% over the next couple of quarters — well above the 2% target. One member, Dave Ramsden, explicitly stated he would have voted for a cut if not for the conflict.

The ECB also held with a warning that the outlook was "significantly more uncertain." The Bank of Japan held at 0.75% by an 8-1 vote; hawkish dissenter Hajime Takata pushed for an immediate raise to 1%.

Interest rates cannot reopen the Strait of Hormuz. They cannot rebuild Qatar's gas infrastructure. What this week's coordinated hold actually communicates is: we will watch and wait, and we are not going to make a bad situation worse. The real monetary policy action of the coming months won't be in rate decisions — it will be in communications and inflation expectations management. For the BoE specifically, the uncomfortable subtext is that a significant cohort would have cut last week were it not for the war. The moment the guns quiet, expect a rapid dovish pivot.

Pakistan vs. Afghanistan — The War Nobody's Watching

Pakistan and Afghanistan agreed to a temporary ceasefire on March 18 to mark Eid al-Fitr, brokered by Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and Turkey. It was the first pause in fighting since late February, when Pakistan declared "open war" on the Taliban government following terrorist attacks inside Pakistan attributed to the TTP.

What the ceasefire masks is scale. Pakistan's Operation Ghazab Lil Haq, launched February 27, has involved sustained airstrikes on Kabul, Kandahar, Nangarhar, and Paktia. By mid-March, Pakistan claimed over 640 Taliban fighters killed, 855 wounded, and 243 border outposts destroyed. UNAMA reported at least 75 civilian deaths in the first two weeks and over 115,000 displaced.

China's concern is nakedly self-interested: instability on Pakistan's western border threatens the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor. CSIS analysis notes that Pakistan's February escalation crossed a qualitative threshold — rather than targeting just TTP camps, it put Taliban military headquarters directly in the target list.

Did You Know?

The Pakistan–Afghanistan border, the Durand Line, was drawn in 1893 by British official Sir Henry Mortimer Durand. Afghanistan has never formally recognised it as an international boundary — making it one of the most legally contested borders in the world.

Northern Ireland: When Global Shocks Hit a Regional Vulnerability

Heating Oil Prices Doubled. 62% of Homes Use It. Stormont Has No Budget Line.

The Iran war arrived on Northern Ireland's doorstep in a very specific form: heating oil. Unlike Great Britain, where the gas grid dominates home heating, approximately 62.5% of Northern Ireland households heat their homes using oil — the highest share of any UK nation. When global prices spiked 40%+ following the Strait's closure, NI experienced the full unmediated pass-through.

The Consumer Council reported that the average cost of 500 litres jumped from £307 to £556 in a single week — an 81% increase. For 900 litres (a typical annual fill), prices went from £536 to £948. There is no price cap on heating oil. Rural households have no practical alternative.

NI Economy Minister Caoimhe Archibald escalated to UK Energy Secretary Ed Miliband, acknowledging the Executive lacked the financial resources to act unilaterally. Chancellor Reeves indicated further measures were under consideration but stopped short of committing funds — explicitly telling MPs there would be no direct public subsidy for heating oil customers.

Northern Ireland's dual-market access under the Windsor Framework has made it an economic outperformer — 2.9% annual growth vs the UK average of 1.3%; median wage up 7.4%. But that structural advantage coexists with a structural vulnerability: the region's almost unique dependence on unregulated heating oil means a war in the Persian Gulf can spike the winter fuel bill for a rural family in Fermanagh by 80% with no regulatory cushion. That's not just an energy policy problem — it's a political vulnerability that Alliance, Sinn Féin, and the DUP will all be competing to own.

Did You Know?

About a fifth of all oil consumed globally each day ordinarily transits the Strait of Hormuz — a waterway at its narrowest just 33km wide. Traffic moves in designated lanes: one inbound, one outbound. The entire bottleneck through which the world's energy security threads is narrower than the distance from Belfast to Lisburn.

Pixel & Byte

Your Switch 2 Just Got Better While You Slept

Nintendo shipped firmware version 22.0.0 on March 16, and buried three lines down from the usual "general system stability improvements" is the headline feature: Handheld Mode Boost. Enable it in Settings → System → Nintendo Switch Software Handling, and compatible Switch (original) games will now run at the same performance level they'd achieve docked — while you're playing in handheld mode.

This matters more than it sounds. The Switch 2's handheld hardware was always more capable than what Nintendo let it run at for backwards-compatible titles, presumably to protect battery life and manage thermals. Early testing by Commonrealm on YouTube confirms genuine performance gains, though results vary by game.

The rest of the update according to Nintendo Life isn't filler either. GameChat now lets you invite friends who haven't finished setup. Airplane Mode gets individual toggles for Bluetooth, Wi-Fi, and NFC. There's a friend notes feature — your annotations, not visible to them. And you can rewind/fast-forward 10 seconds in eShop videos with ZL/ZR.

Family flag: Super Mario Bros. Wonder – Switch 2 Edition + Meetup in Bellabel Park drops March 26. The Switch 2 version adds a whole new Bellabel Park mode for local co-op, plus Rosalina as a playable character. This is the co-op family game of the moment.

Crimson Desert: Seven Years in the Oven, Mixed First Day Out

Pearl Abyss launched Crimson Desert on March 19 — PC, PS5, and Xbox — and the numbers are staggering: 2 million copies sold in a single day, peak concurrent Steam players approaching 250,000. Seven years in development. A vast open-world action RPG set in the war-torn fantasy continent of Pywel.

The problem is the reception. Steam currently sits at "Mixed," with the most consistent complaints landing on cumbersome controls and graphical performance issues on both PS5 and PC. Pearl Abyss has responded promising to "work to make improvements quickly." The bones of an impressive game appear to be there; the question is how fast they can sand the rough edges.

If you have GeForce NOW, this is exactly the kind of game to try before committing 150GB locally. Worth watching the patch trajectory over April before buying on Steam.

Also launched this week: Death Stranding 2: On the Beach arrived on PC on March 19. If you missed it on PS5 last year, Kojima's post-apocalyptic courier epic is exactly the kind of slow, strange, meditative game that rewards a Steam Deck session. And Slay the Spire 2 is now in Early Access with the killer addition: co-op for up to four players, with multiplayer-specific cards and team synergies.

LEGO Smart Play: Two Weeks In, The Verdict Is "Kids Love It, Prices Hurt"

LEGO's Smart Play system — announced at CES in January, launched March 1 — has now had two weeks in actual homes, and IGN's community roundup gives a clear read: families with kids who actually play LEGO are delighted; adult collectors are watching from a safe distance.

The Smart Brick is a modified 2x4 brick packed with a custom chip, motion sensor, gyroscope, pressure-sensing microphone, speaker, LEDs, and a light/colour sensor — all connecting to standard studs. It reacts to movement (swooping X-Wing sounds as you fly it), Smart Tags (telling the brick what it is), and Smart Minifigures (Vader gets his breathing, Luke his lightsaber hum). No screens, no app required.

The launch wave includes eight Star Wars sets themed around the Original Trilogy. Jay's Brick Blog flags the main friction point: add-on sets like the Millennium Falcon ($99.99, 885 pieces) don't include a Smart Brick — you need a hub set too. A grandparent buying the Falcon expecting the full experience is going to be confused.

Family flag: For a 10-year-old who likes Star Wars, the $99.99 Red Five X-Wing is the entry point — it includes the Smart Brick, refuelling cart, rebel base station, and enough interactive surface to keep it interesting.

March is also massive for non-Smart LEGO: the Legend of Zelda Ocarina of Time: The Final Battle set ($129.99), LEGO Icons' Sauron's Helmet ($69.99), and the Bridge over a Pond of Water Lilies art set ($249.99). The LEGO Pokémon Venusaur, Charizard and Blastoise triple-build at $649.99 launched in February and is being reviewed with reverence across YouTube.

Did You Know?

The LEGO Smart Brick's onboard chip is smaller than a single LEGO stud — and uses a synthetic sound architecture that generates radically different audio (jet engine, toilet flush, lightsaber hum, explosion) from the same core waveform building blocks. LEGO holds more than twenty patents on the system's technology.

Juventus — The Narrative

It has been, as so often with this Juventus, a week defined by quiet competence rather than explosive theatre. The 1–0 win at Udinese — Francisco Conceição with the winner — was exactly the kind of greasy, unglamorous result that distinguishes title contenders from perennial also-rans.

Juve sit fifth, three points behind Como and seven behind Milan, with a game in hand. What makes this moment peculiar is the backdrop. Luciano Spalletti — back in club management after his World Cup-qualifying humiliation with the Azzurri — has steadied the ship after a truly alarming stretch in February when Juve lost to Galatasaray in the Champions League play-offs 5–7 on aggregate. Since then: four wins in five league outings.

Kenan Yildiz is the story of the season. Nine goals, six assists in 28 appearances, and a list of admirers including Real Madrid, Barcelona, and every major Premier League club. He's 20 years old. Juventus have reportedly identified him as untouchable, alongside Bremer, Locatelli, Kalulu, McKennie, and Conceição.

Dusan Vlahovic remains the soap opera. Talks over a contract extension have quietly begun — a significant reversal for a player widely expected to leave. The key question is salary: Vlahovic currently earns €12 million net, Serie A's highest. Whether this is genuine negotiation or tactical delay remains unclear.

Premier League — Arsenal's Race to Win

Nine points clear with eight games to play. Arsenal, under Mikel Arteta in his fifth full season, are doing the thing they have been threatening to do since 2022: they are closing it out. The gap between Arsenal (70) and City (61) — with City having one game in hand — is theoretically closeable, but City have now spent most of the season watching Arsenal extend the lead and then shrinking it slightly. They haven't overtaken them since September.

Manchester United's 3–1 win over Aston Villa was the kind of performance that reminds you they are a legitimate top-four threat under Ruben Amorim. Casemiro, who at one point looked finished, is playing the best football of his United tenure. Bruno Šeško is converting at a rate that justifies the expensive summer signing.

The race for fourth place — the last Champions League spot — involves Villa (51), Liverpool (49), Chelsea (48), Brentford (45), and Everton (43) all within eight points. It is chaotic and compelling.

At the bottom, Wolves (17 points) are six points adrift and look gone. Burnley (20) and West Ham (29) are in the drop zone.

The Week in Film, TV & Theme Parks

Peaky Blinders: The Immortal Man

After a four-year wait, Tommy Shelby is back — and Steven Knight had the courage to actually end it. Peaky Blinders: The Immortal Man dropped on Netflix on March 20 after a limited cinema run. Shot in Birmingham, directed by Tom Harper, it runs 112 minutes and earns 91% on Rotten Tomatoes, with critics calling it "a satisfying conclusion that also stands tall on its own self-contained terms."

The setup: it's 1940, Birmingham is being bombed, and Tommy has retreated into isolation following Arthur's death. His son Duke (Barry Keoghan, terrific) has taken over the gang — and driven it into the arms of a Nazi agent named Beckett (Tim Roth). Tommy, inevitably, gets dragged back in.

What Knight and Harper do smartly is make this feel like a wartime thriller that happens to star the Blinders, not a nostalgia exercise. The WWII backdrop gives the story moral weight. Murphy is, as ever, magnetic. The film actually ends Tommy's story, definitively, in a way the TV finale deliberately left open. If you've been here since 2013, the payoff is real.

The verdict: Watch it. And note the soundtrack — Grian Chatten of Fontaines DC, Lankum, Nick Cave, Amy Taylor of Amyl and the Sniffers. Knight knows what he's doing with music.

Project Hail Mary — Science Fiction Done Right

Ryan Gosling wakes up alone in deep space with no memory, his crewmates dead, and the fate of Earth resting on whatever he can piece together. Based on Andy Weir's novel, directed by Phil Lord and Christopher Miller, Project Hail Mary opened March 20 and is exactly the kind of crowd-pleasing, genuinely intelligent blockbuster that feels rare.

The alien co-star (a puppet, not CG, and one critics are already calling "out of this world") is a delight. This is the film to take a curious 10-year-old to, full stop. It's playing in IMAX and standard cinemas now.

The verdict: The best new film in cinemas this week. Smart enough for adults, accessible enough for kids, and it doesn't talk down to either.

Also Noted: The Bride!

Maggie Gyllenhaal's follow-up to The Lost Daughter is... a lot. Jessie Buckley plays a reanimated Frankenstein's bride in 1930s Chicago; Christian Bale is the Monster; Annette Bening is the scientist. It's a revisionist steampunk glam-punk gangster romance quasi-musical — and while that description is chaotic, so is the film. Buckley is extraordinary and Gyllenhaal is swinging for the fences. Worth seeing if you like cinema that argues with itself.

Star Wars: Maul Returns

On March 16, Lucasfilm dropped the full official trailer for Star Wars: Maul – Shadow Lord, the new animated series premiering on Disney+ on April 6 as a two-episode event. Ten episodes, wrapping on May the 4th (naturally). The style is unlike anything Star Wars animation has done before: watercolour and paint-on-glass, genuinely cinematic, noir-inflected, and dark.

The story picks up after The Clone Wars, with Maul (Sam Witwer, still the definitive performance) laying low on Janix, attempting to rebuild his criminal syndicate. The Empire arrives — led by Inquisitors — and Maul's response is to find a weapon: Devon Izara, a disillusioned Jedi Padawan who survived Order 66.

Created by Dave Filoni — now confirmed as co-president of Lucasfilm — this looks pulpy, fast-paced, and genuinely exciting. The Inquisitors, a morally compromised Maul, a Jedi survivor with nowhere to go: all the ingredients.

The Mandalorian & Grogu: Pedro Pascal's comments about removing his helmet suggest the film will navigate the creed in interesting ways. Also confirmed in the trailer: Sigourney Weaver as Colonel Ward, Jeremy Allen White as Rotta the Hutt, and Martin Scorsese voicing an Ardennian fry cook (yes, really). Opens exclusively in theatres and IMAX on May 22.

Fitness, Running & the Body

Dan John: The Philosophy That Never Gets Old

Dan John is one of those coaches who publishes fresh content by restating ancient truths in ways that cut through the noise. His March 2026 video on two-day kettlebell programming reinforces the themes he has been developing across thirty-plus years: simplicity, consistency, movement patterns over exercises.

The framework underpinning everything Dan John does is the five movement patterns: Push, Pull, Hinge, Squat, Loaded Carry. If your training hits all five, you're covering the bases. Gaps in any pattern are gaps in the athlete.

The "Even Easier Strength" method is particularly relevant for anyone balancing kettlebells, gym work, and running. The core rule: "Never miss a rep — never even get close to struggling." Pick five lifts (one per pattern), do them every session, stay under ten reps, rotate variations every two weeks. Dan John broke his lifetime incline bench press record — 315 for a double — on workout 22, having never once trained to failure.

The Humane Burpee remains one of his most elegant single-kettlebell solutions: 15 swings / 5 goblet squats / 5 push-ups, descending (15/4/4, 15/3/3, 15/2/2, 15/1/1). As he puts it in Attempts: "If your life is crazy, make your training boring."

The Practical Takeaway

A gym session, a kettlebell session, and two easy runs per week is not undertraining. For a non-competitive athlete, it's a reasonable programme that covers all the bases. Dan John would look at push/pull/hinge/squat/carry three times a week and say: that is enough. In fact, that might be exactly enough.

Zone 2: The Runs That Feel Too Slow

Zone 2 has gone from elite endurance secret to near-mainstream, and a January 2026 Runner's World piece lays out three diagnostic signs you need more of it:

  1. Cardiac drift — your easy run feels fine for 30 minutes, then breathing creeps up even though pace is steady. A 6–10% heart rate drift signals aerobic underfitting.
  2. PR gaps — if you can run 400m in 90 seconds, you should manage a 5K in about 26 minutes. Closer to 30? Aerobic engine needs work.
  3. Poor recovery — previously attainable paces feel out of reach; you dread workouts rather than looking forward to them.

Zone 2 is roughly 60–70% of maximum heart rate — the "can hold a conversation" pace. The Runners Digest guide recommends: for a 4-run week, two Zone 2 sessions of 45–60 minutes, one interval session, and a long run mostly in Zone 2. For 3 runs, two Zone 2 and one harder session.

Dan John has specifically called out "strength + Zone 2 as the ideal pairing" for general health — the two things that complement rather than compete. Squats three times a week, Zone 2 running twice a week: a remarkably complete programme.

The Harry Styles Marathon Story

Genuinely interesting, this. Runner's World's spring cover feature documents Harry Styles crediting Haruki Murakami's What I Talk About When I Talk About Running with convincing him he could run a marathon — which he did in Tokyo in 2025 (3:24) and then Berlin six months later in 2:59:13. If you're not familiar with Murakami's memoir: the novelist runs daily, has completed dozens of marathons, and views running as inseparable from writing. Worth knowing about.

The Marathon Just Got Faster, Again

On March 15, Ethiopian runner Fotyen Tesfay ran 2:10:51 at the Zurich Barcelona Marathon — the second-fastest women's marathon in history, on her debut. She was 57 seconds off Ruth Chepngetich's disputed world record. As recently as 2019, no woman had broken Paula Radcliffe's seemingly immortal 2:15:25 mark. In seven years, eight women have. The women's marathon has been rewritten entirely.

The 47% Finding

A Florida Atlantic University study published in The American Journal of Medicine found adults with the highest intake of ultra-processed foods had a 47% higher risk of heart attack or stroke — even after controlling for age, smoking, and income. The dataset: 4,787 US adults from the NHANES survey. Senior author Charles Hennekens compared the potential impact of reducing ultra-processed food consumption to anti-smoking campaigns. Not recycled advice — genuinely new data with a striking effect size.

Did You Know?

The kettlebell goblet squat was invented by Dan John. He came up with both the exercise and the name — holding a kettlebell at the chest like a goblet — and popularised it as a teaching tool for squat mechanics. It's now one of the most widely coached squat variations in the world.

This Week in History

The Ides of March, 44 BC

Et Tu, Brute?

The fifteenth of March has been resonating ominously across two thousand years. Julius Caesar, dictator of Rome, was warned by a soothsayer weeks before the date that bore his fate. He ignored the warning. His wife Calpurnia dreamed of his murder the night before and begged him to stay home. He was persuaded out of the house anyway — by Decimus Brutus, one of the men about to kill him.

That single detail deserves to sit with you. It wasn't Brutus of "Et tu, Brute?" fame who got Caesar out of bed on the Ides of March — it was another Brutus entirely, a man Caesar trusted so completely that the historian Nicolaus of Damascus records Decimus admonishing Caesar for "listening to the fears of women and foolish chatter." And Caesar went.

At the Theatre of Pompey — not the Senate chamber, as is commonly misremembered — somewhere between sixty and sixty-six senators were waiting, daggers concealed beneath their togas. One senator, Tillius Cimber, approached Caesar with a petition, pulling at his clothes. This was the signal. Servilius Casca struck first. Caesar managed to wound him with a stylus. Then the others closed in.

He was stabbed twenty-three times and died at the base of a statue of Pompey — the man he had defeated in civil war, whose theatre now served as the Senate's temporary meeting place. The symmetry would have delighted a playwright. Shakespeare, writing sixteen centuries later, couldn't resist it.

The irony the conspirators failed to appreciate: by killing Caesar, they didn't restore the Republic. As the historian Appian observed, the difference between dictator and king was "a mere quibble." Caesar's death accelerated the Republic's collapse, delivered power to Octavian (who became Augustus), and launched the Imperial era the constitution was designed to prevent. Through Eternity Tours puts it cleanly: "Ironically the assassination accelerated rather than halted the collapse of Republican Rome."

Caesar had declared himself dictator for life just a month before his death. The men who killed him believed they were saving the Republic. History's verdict is that they merely changed its executioner.

Did You Know?

The Roman calendar that gave us the Ides of March was organised around three named days per month — Kalends (1st), Nones (5th or 7th), and Ides (13th or 15th). The Ides originally marked the full moon. Caesar's own calendar reform — the Julian Calendar, introduced 45 BC — was arguably his most enduring legacy, forming the basis of the Gregorian calendar still in use today.

Also This Week in History

  1. March 20, 1413 — Henry IV of England dies in the Jerusalem Chamber at Westminster Abbey, fulfilling a prophecy that he would die in Jerusalem. He had spent his reign convincing himself this meant crusade. It meant a room. (Britannica)
  2. March 21, 1556 — Thomas Cranmer, first Protestant Archbishop of Canterbury, is burned at the stake by Queen Mary I. At the moment of execution, he thrust his right hand — the hand that had signed his recantation of Protestantism — directly into the flames, so it would be destroyed first. A final, remarkable act of defiance from a man who had spent his last months wishing he were braver. (Historic UK)
  3. March 17 — St Patrick's Day. Patrick himself was British, likely Romano-British, abducted by Irish raiders as a teenager and enslaved in Ireland before escaping, converting, and returning as a missionary. The shamrock? Almost certainly a later addition. The parades? Invented in eighteenth-century America by Irish emigrants.
