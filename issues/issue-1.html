Bandwidth is finite. That is the thread running through everything this week — from Washington's diplomatic switchboard, overwhelmed by Iran and deaf to Ukraine, to the Bank of England's nine-to-zero paralysis, to 92 million Iranians cut from the internet at one percent of capacity. When every channel is saturated, the signal that gets through is the loudest, not the most important. The things that dropped out of the queue this week — a hospital strike that killed 408 people, a record day of Russian casualties, a planet made of magma — deserved better than silence.

By Sunday March 15, the US-Israeli campaign against Iran was into day sixteen. The operational picture had shifted: after the opening phase of leadership decapitation — Supreme Leader Khamenei killed in the initial strikes, former president Ahmadinejad initially reported dead but later found alive — the US moved to targeting Iran's economic chokepoints. On Friday March 13, President Trump ordered strikes on Kharg Island, the terminal that handles roughly 90% of Iran's crude oil exports. Trump told NBC's Kristen Welker the strikes had "totally demolished" much of the island and warned of more. The Strait of Hormuz — responsible for around 20% of global oil supply — remained under intense pressure.

Iran was not passive. Saudi Arabia intercepted six missiles and 34 drones in a single overnight salvo on March 15. Bahrain has brought down more than 125 missiles and over 200 drones since hostilities began. The UAE suffered a drone strike at Fujairah that disrupted oil-loading operations. Iranian ballistic missiles had already struck Al Udeid Air Base in Qatar — the largest US military base in the region. US forces torpedoed the Iranian frigate IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka, killing at least 87 of the 180 aboard. The war has cost the United States over $10 billion in its first two weeks, with at least six American soldiers dead.

The real story, beyond the kinetic theatre, is the diplomatic geometry. Russia is providing Iran with intelligence to help target US ships and assets — turning this from a bilateral affair into a test of great-power alignment. China is in talks with Iran over safe passage for tankers through Hormuz. The EU's Costa and von der Leyen are video-conferencing Gulf leaders. US Treasury Secretary Bessent was in Paris preparing for a potential Trump-Xi summit. And at the UN Security Council, the US and allies clashed with Russia and China over Iran's nuclear programme — confirming that even the shared architecture of non-proliferation governance has fractured.

The Angle

This is no longer a war about Iran's nuclear programme. It is the first open test, since the Cold War, of whether the US can fight a major regional conflict while the Russia-China axis actively assists the other side. Every meeting, every ceasefire feeler, every IAEA inspection clause now happens in that shadow.

Central Banks Freeze: The Day Four Economies Said "We Don't Know"

Thursday March 19 produced one of the most extraordinary single days in recent central banking history. The Bank of England voted 9-0 to hold at 3.75% — the first unanimous decision since September 2021. Markets had priced in a 7-2 split expecting two members to push for cuts. The BoE now warns CPI could hit 3.5% by Q3, up from 2.1% just weeks earlier, entirely driven by the Iran-war energy shock. Two-year gilt yields spiked 33 basis points to 4.43%. Market pricing shifted: from two cuts expected this year to two potential hikes.

The ECB held at 2.00%, raised its 2026 inflation forecast to 2.6%, and cut growth to 0.9%. The Bank of Japan held at 0.75%. The Federal Reserve had held the previous day. S&P Global cut 2026 growth forecasts "pretty much across the board" and raised inflation projections. US new home sales collapsed 17.6% to 587,000 annualised — the lowest since October 2022.

The Angle

This is stagflation through the mechanism of a single conflict. Policymakers who spent 2024 celebrating their navigation of post-pandemic inflation are watching energy prices tear through their models again — not from a supply shock they can dismiss as "transitory," but from a war their governments are either participating in or being pressured to join. The BoE's unanimous hold is the political signal as much as the monetary one.

Did You Know?

The Strait of Hormuz is just 33 kilometres wide at its narrowest point. Through it flows roughly one-fifth of the world's entire oil supply — about 21 million barrels per day. There is no alternative route for most Gulf oil exports.

Ukraine: The War Washington Forgot

Ukrainian forces eliminated 1,710 Russian soldiers in a single day this week — the highest single-day casualty figure in 2026. Drone operators alone accounted for approximately 900 kills in a 36-hour window. Russia has lost close to 100,000 soldiers in the first three months of 2026, with Zelensky claiming 90% of those casualties were caused by drones. And yet: the Kremlin announced a pause in peace negotiations — coinciding precisely with the moment Trump's bandwidth became consumed by the Iran war. Russia plans to recruit an additional 400,000 soldiers in 2026. Washington postponed the latest settlement talks.

The record daily losses and Ukraine's effective removal from Washington's diplomatic agenda are two sides of the same coin. Russia is simultaneously taking catastrophic casualties and being handed a strategic gift: no pressure to negotiate.

Pakistan–Afghanistan: 408 Dead, and Nobody Looked Up

Since late February, Pakistan and Afghanistan have been in open military conflict. This week's pivotal moment: Afghan officials alleged that a Pakistani airstrike hit the Omid Addiction Treatment Hospital in Kabul on Monday March 16, killing at least 408 people and wounding 265 others — the deadliest single attack since the conflict's escalation. Pakistan denied the claim, saying it targeted an ammunition depot. The UN's OHCHR had already confirmed 289 Afghan civilians, including 104 children and 59 women, killed or injured before the hospital figures were added. On Wednesday, Saudi Arabia, Turkey and Qatar brokered a temporary ceasefire for Eid al-Fitr.

Two Muslim-majority states at war over the consequences of a twenty-year Western project, playing out in near-total Western media silence. The hospital strike allegation — 408 people killed in a single building — would have been the defining news event of any ordinary week. It barely registered.

Northern Ireland: £35 Per Household

Two-thirds of NI households depend on home heating oil — a commodity with no price regulation and no equivalent to the energy price cap that protects gas and electricity customers in Great Britain. Within a single week in early March, the average cost of 500 litres jumped from £307 to £556 — an 81% increase. On Monday, the UK government announced £17 million for Northern Ireland from a £53 million UK-wide support package. With roughly 500,000 oil-heating homes, the allocation works out to about £35 per household. First Minister Michelle O'Neill called it a "slap in the face." The same week, the Independent Remuneration Board confirmed a 27% pay rise for Stormont MLAs — from £53,000 to £67,200.

Arsenal and the Title They Finally Deserve

Arsenal lead by nine points. Their 2-0 win over Everton on March 14 was followed by City dropping points for the second consecutive match — 1-1 at West Ham — and the gap went from seven to nine. NBC Sports called it "the night Arsenal won the Premier League" — bold, but not unreasonable. With seven matches remaining and an April 19 showdown at the Etihad still to come, it isn't mathematically over, but Arsenal's squad depth, form, and mental resilience suggest this is theirs to lose. They haven't won a league title since 2004. Mikel Arteta, who has had his patience tested across multiple near-misses, deserves enormous credit.

Juve: Two Clean Sheets and a Midfield Problem

Juventus ground out a narrow 1-0 win at Udinese — Jérémie Boga's third consecutive Serie A goal — making it two wins and two clean sheets in their last two. After the Champions League exit to Galatasaray (7-5 on aggregate, a second-leg comeback that fell agonisingly short in extra time), back-to-back league wins matter. Tonight they host Sassuolo. Three points would take them to 56, within range of fourth-placed Como on 54. Kenan Yıldız has 9 goals and 6 assists this season — the first Juventus player since the league expanded to 20 teams to register 15+ goal contributions before turning 21. Vlahović is doubtful with a tendon issue; Jonathan David starts.

Spalletti wants a midfielder (Ederson from Atalanta, €35-40m), a striker, and potentially a free-transfer marquee name — Bernardo Silva from City is on the radar. A squad overhaul of four to five players is planned for the summer.

The Take

Juventus's structural problem is not the manager — it's the midfield. Spalletti has been handed a squad whose creative axis is energetic but not commanding. None of Koopmeiners, McKennie, or Thuram dictates play the way Brozović or Jorginho did at their best. Until they sign someone who can genuinely run the show, Juve will keep winning scrappy 1-0s when on form and shipping 3-3s against mid-table Roma when they're not. The Yıldız renaissance is real, but you cannot build a title-challenging midfield around Weston McKennie.

Maul Shadow Lord: A Trailer That Looks Like Nothing Else in the Franchise

The official trailer for Star Wars: Maul – Shadow Lord dropped Monday, confirming its April 6 Disney+ premiere as a two-episode event (final episodes on May 4 — Star Wars Day). Set after The Clone Wars, the 10-episode animated series follows Darth Maul rebuilding his criminal empire during the early days of the Galactic Empire. Malazan's Endgame Is Confirmed

Books

The End of the World's Longest Fantasy

At the February launch event for No Life Forsaken at Munro's Books in Victoria, Steven Erikson confirmed what the Malazan community has been both hoping for and dreading. Walk in Shadow — the final book of the Kharkanas Trilogy — is very close to hitting the publisher's desk. Legacies of Betrayal (Witness #3) is tentatively dated October 1, 2026 — the book almost entirely focused on marine squads. Witness #4 is tentatively titled The Last God, in which Karsa will appear and a certain question about Kruppe's true nature will be addressed. There is, Erikson mentioned, a non-zero chance Kallor gets his own book — the room apparently erupted.

The headline, though, is final: after the current series finish — Witness, Kharkanas, B&KB, Path to Ascendancy — both Erikson and Esslemont will be done with the Malazan world. Ian Esslemont's fifth Path to Ascendancy book is also nearly ready. The broadest, strangest, most demanding fantasy series of the modern era has a definite, approaching endpoint.

Dan John's Simple Two-Day Kettlebell Programme

Dan John posted a new video on March 4 that distils decades of training philosophy into what may be his most practical programming template yet. The structure is a two-day split — one day focused on press/row/ab wheel, the other on RDL/front squat/farmer carry. Both movement categories appear in each session, but one is the "focus" (sets of 15, pushing reps) and the other is "practice" (3×8 with exact 60-second rest periods). The key cue, vintage Dan John: "You're not pushing, you're practicing."

The brilliance is in what it doesn't ask you to do. No complex periodisation. No percentage-based loading. No tracking apps. Two days, six movements, timed rests. The focus/practice split ensures you're always building one quality while maintaining the other. For anyone who owns a kettlebell and wants a programme that could run for months without modification, this is the one.

Zone 2: The Conversation That Won't End

Zone 2 training — 60-70% of max heart rate, conversational pace — remains the dominant methodology in recreational running in 2026. The 80/20 structure (80% easy, 20% hard) is the practitioner default. For a four-run week: two Zone 2 sessions of 45-60 minutes, one quality session (intervals or threshold), and one long run mostly at easy pace. The key mistake is drifting into the "grey zone" — too slow for quality work, too fast for aerobic benefit. The talk test remains the simplest check: can you speak full sentences without gasping?

The current nuance: some exercise scientists are pushing back on Zone 2 as the only mechanism. Ross Tucker and Kolie Moore argue that serious athletes programme as much high intensity as they need, then fill with low intensity — the volume of easy work matters more than hitting a precise heart rate zone.

Hybrid Training: The Balance Principle

Jake Dearden, HYROX pro (55:44 PB; 2:22 marathon in January 2026), spoke to Red Bull this week about the myth that running and strength training don't coexist. His framework for 4-5 sessions per week: one HYROX-style session, one threshold run (800m repeats), one slow long run, one weights session. The governing rule: reduce gym intensity when running volume rises. Don't chase PRs in both lifts and sprints simultaneously. Maintain strength during run-focused blocks; maintain your endurance base during strength blocks.

This Week in History

The Ides of March: The Conspiracy That Killed a Republic

Julius Caesar had crossed the Rubicon in 49 BC, won the civil war, and been appointed dictator perpetuo — dictator in perpetuity — in early 44 BC. He was days away from departing Rome on a campaign against the Parthians. The Senate meeting of March 15 was the last before he left.

The conspiracy was led by senators Marcus Junius Brutus and Gaius Cassius Longinus, with roughly 60 participants. They chose the Senate House deliberately — killing a tyrant before the full Senate would look like a noble act, not a political plot. In the days before, Caesar had been warned. A seer named Spurinna predicted his life was in danger no later than the Ides of March. On the morning of March 15, as Caesar passed the seer on the way to the Senate House, he called out playfully: "Well, the Ides of March have come!" The seer replied: "Aye, the Ides have come — but they are not yet gone."

Mark Antony was intercepted at the door and kept outside. Inside, the conspirators surrounded Caesar under the pretence of a petition. Casca struck first — a glancing blow to the neck. Caesar grabbed Casca's arm. Then it was all motion: Cassius slashed his face, others struck from every direction. Blinded by blood, Caesar fell against the pedestal of Pompey's statue. He was stabbed 23 times. Only the second wound — to the ribs — was fatal, according to what may be the earliest known autopsy report in history.

Brutus and his companions marched through Rome: "People of Rome, we are once again free!" They were met with silence. Citizens had locked their doors. Rather than restoring the Republic, the assassination triggered civil war. Caesar was posthumously deified. His great-nephew Octavian avenged the murder, hunted down the conspirators, and eventually became Augustus — the first Roman Emperor. The Republic they died to save never recovered. A conspiracy designed to preserve freedom that instead ended it — the gap between what we intend and what we cause.
