Leo Returns — and Sets the Record Straight

Pope Leo XIV — Wikimedia Commons / Public Domain
0Days travelling
0Countries visited
0Faithful at Douala Mass
0Flights taken

Pope Leo XIV landed back in Rome on 24 April having completed the most ambitious papal trip in years — 11 days, four African countries, more than 11,000 miles flown, and a pointed in-flight press conference that made clear he has no interest in playing the diplomatic game the way his predecessors did.

The journey took him from Algeria's ancient ruins of Hippo Regius — site of St Augustine's final years, a saint the pope calls himself "a son of" — through the armed separatist heartland of Cameroon's Bamenda region, where local factions declared a three-day ceasefire to allow safe passage, to diamond country in Angola, to a stadium Mass attended by 30,000 in Equatorial Guinea. Each stop carried a version of the same message: peace, dignity, an end to exploitation.

Key Stops
  • Annaba, Algeria — Ruins of Hippo Regius, St Augustine's basilica
  • Bamenda, Cameroon — Armed separatist zone; ceasefire declared for visit
  • Douala, Cameroon — Mass for ~120,000 faithful
  • Saurimo, Angola — Diamond mine region; nursing home visit
  • Mongomo, Eq. Guinea — Basilica of the Immaculate Conception Mass

What made the trip genuinely unusual was the tension it created with Washington. Leo's consistent anti-war messaging — explicitly aimed at the situation in Iran — prompted a public dispute with President Trump. On the papal plane home, Leo addressed it head-on, telling reporters that his primary purpose was pastoral, not political, but adding that "as a pastor, I cannot be in favour of war — too many innocents have died." He did not name Trump. He didn't need to.

The visit to Angola carried particular symbolic weight. The Church of Our Lady of Muxima, which Leo attended, sits within a fortified complex that once formed part of the Portuguese slave trade — a fact whose history the Vatican did not shy away from. The first American pope, himself heir to a complex national history, praying at a shrine built in a slaving fort on the continent most affected by that trade: the symbolism wrote itself.

Back in Rome, the question the Vatican is now navigating is what comes next. Leo's exchanges with Trump — who has invoked the pope's opposition to the Iran war as evidence of anti-American bias — are sharpening into something resembling a genuine theological and political contest over who gets to claim the moral language of peace. Leo, for his part, seems unbothered. "I would go back," he told journalists, "to the importance of understanding the primary purpose of travel." He means: don't read state visits as geopolitical manoeuvres. Whether anyone in Washington is listening is another matter.

The $166 Billion Owed Back

$166bn
The total owed by the US government to approximately 330,000 importers after the Supreme Court struck down IEEPA tariffs in February

On Monday 20 April, US Customs and Border Protection launched the Consolidated Administration and Processing of Entries (CAPE) portal — an online system through which businesses can file for refunds on tariffs collected under emergency powers that the Supreme Court ruled unconstitutional in February. The scale is without precedent in US trade history: the outstanding balance sits at roughly $166 billion, accruing around $650 million in interest each month.

The tariffs in question were levied under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act — the legal tool the Trump administration used in 2025 to implement sweeping import duties. Courts had been chipping away at their legal basis for months, with the Supreme Court's February ruling landing the killing blow. Over 3,000 companies — including FedEx and Costco — had already filed lawsuits seeking recovery before the portal even opened.

The Angle: Approved payments won't arrive for another 60–90 days, and only importers who directly paid the tariffs are eligible — not the consumers who absorbed the price increases. The $166 billion flows back through the supply chain; the millions of Americans who paid more for imported goods see none of it directly.

Mexico's government moved fast in the same week, signalling it was open to an early bilateral deal on steel, aluminium, and autos ahead of any broader USMCA revision — a sign that trading partners are repositioning themselves rapidly as the trade landscape shifts again. India-US talks also began in Washington, with both sides eyeing a framework before the end of Q2.

    Harissa Chicken & Chickpea Tagine

    This is batch cooking as it should be: one pot, four portions of proper food, a sauce that improves overnight, and a meal that genuinely doesn't feel like prep. It's a BBC Good Food recipe that hits the macro targets without the blank expression of "another chicken and rice." The harissa marinade does the heavy lifting on flavour, the chickpeas bulk out the protein further, and couscous keeps the carbs manageable.

    The trick is the two-step simmer. The chicken braises with stock, tomatoes, and raisins for 45 minutes covered, then the chickpeas go in uncovered for a final 15 minutes to thicken the sauce. Don't rush that final stage — a loose tagine is a sad tagine.

    456kcal / serving
    52gprotein
    4portions
    Chicken tagine — Wikimedia Commons

    The Recipe

    PrepCookServesDifficulty
    10 min (+ 30 min marinate)1 hr 15 min4Easy
    Ingredients
    • 800g skinless boneless chicken thighs, cut into large chunks
    • 1 tbsp harissa paste
    • 1 tbsp vegetable oil
    • 1 large onion, finely sliced
    • 1 tsp each: ground cinnamon, cumin, turmeric
    • 500ml chicken stock
    • 400g can chopped tomatoes
    • 100g raisins
    • 400g can chickpeas, drained
    • 250g couscous (to serve)
    • Small handful mint leaves (to serve)
    Method
    1. Toss chicken with harissa, cover and refrigerate 20–30 minutes.
    2. Brown chicken in oil 2–3 min, remove. Soften onion 8–10 min, stir in spices.
    3. Return chicken with stock, tomatoes, raisins. Season, bring to boil, reduce heat. Simmer covered 45 min.
    4. Add chickpeas, simmer uncovered 15 min until sauce thickens. Serve over couscous with mint.
    Storage & Reheating
    • Keeps in the fridge for 4 days in airtight containers.
    • Freezes well for up to 3 months — freeze without the couscous.
    • Reheat gently on the hob with a splash of water, or 3 minutes in the microwave.
    • Scales easily to 8 portions — use a large casserole and double everything.
    • Family note: tone the harissa down slightly for younger palates; add a dollop of yoghurt to serve.

    Seven Days Out: What the Science Says About the Final Week

    • A 10k race requires minimal in-race fuelling — the pre-race meal does most of the work
    • Reducing volume while maintaining intensity outperforms full rest in the final week
    • 60% of recreational runners arrive at the start line with muscle damage from insufficient taper

    The race-week taper is one of the most counterintuitive periods in endurance training. Everything the body has been building for weeks feels suddenly at risk, and the natural response — to squeeze in one more hard session, one more long run, something to "check the work" — is exactly the wrong one.

    Research from the University of Limburg tracked muscle condition in recreational runners through a marathon build-up and into their taper. Sixty percent arrived at the start line with measurable muscle damage and depleted glycogen stores. The cause wasn't the race — it was the training. Tapering too little is common; tapering too much leaves runners flat on race day. The goal is a progressive reduction in volume while maintaining intensity, not a full shutdown.

    Sample Race-Week Protocol
    • Mon: Easy 30–40 min run
    • Tue: Short session at race pace (5 × 400m, full recovery)
    • Wed: Rest or 20 min easy
    • Thu: 20–25 min easy
    • Fri: Rest. Begin carb focus.
    • Sat: Shakeout — 15 min easy + 4 × 15-sec strides
    • Sun: Race day

    For a 10k specifically — a distance most runners complete in under 60 minutes — in-race fuelling is largely irrelevant. Glycogen stores, properly loaded in the final 48 hours, will not deplete within that duration. The focus shifts entirely to the pre-race meal (3–4 hours before, tested food, nothing new), race-morning hydration, and a good night's sleep on Friday. The night before the race is always disrupted; that's fine. The sleep earlier in the week is what actually matters.

    Coach Greg McMillan makes a useful reframe: don't think about it as a taper. Think of it as a peak. The training is done — this week is optimisation. Maintain run frequency if you normally run four or five times a week. Cut daily volume by 20–30 minutes rather than half. Do one session at race pace — not for fitness, but to remind the nervous system what race effort feels like. Then trust the work.

    Did You Know? The term "taper madness" — the anxiety, phantom aches, and restlessness runners feel during reduced training — is a documented psychological phenomenon. The symptoms are caused by a drop in endorphin-driven mood regulation, not by illness or injury.

    Nutrition this week: no dramatic changes. Increase carbohydrate proportion gradually from Thursday (aiming for around 70–75% of calories from carbs on Friday and Saturday). Stay hydrated. Avoid anything that's been untested on a long run — new pre-workout, unusual foods, high-fibre novelties. The week before a race is not the time to experiment. Sources: Runner's World, McMillan Running, Team RunRun

    21 April, 753 BC: The Traditional Foundation of Rome

    753 BC
    The Capitoline Wolf with Romulus and Remus — Wikimedia Commons

    On 21 April, by tradition, Romulus drew a line in the dirt on the Palatine Hill and called it a city. The date has been commemorated for over two millennia — first by Roman historians, later as the Natalis Urbis (Birthday of the City) — and every subsequent Roman emperor, republic, aqueduct, law code, and gladiatorial bout traces its lineage back to this moment. Or so the story goes.

    The founding myth is remarkable not for its plausibility — two brothers raised by a she-wolf and fathered by Mars, the god of war, is a conspicuously heroic origin story — but for its longevity. Romulus and Remus were used to explain Rome's character: martial, fratricidal, ambitious. The killing of Remus for jumping over his brother's newly dug city wall explains, in miniature, why Rome was the kind of civilisation that would one day execute people for crossing borders it had decided were sacred.

    Every empire begins with a line in the dirt — and a decision about who may cross it.

    — Observation on the Romulus founding myth

    The date of 21 April 753 BC was calculated by the Roman scholar Varro in the first century BC — working backwards from known historical events. Modern archaeology is largely agnostic: there's evidence of settlement on the Palatine from at least the 10th century BC, continuous growth through the 8th, but no single founding moment. Cities don't begin on a Tuesday. The myth does the political work that history cannot.

    What the Romans themselves believed changed over time. By the late Republic, the Romulus legend was as politically contested as the founding itself — used to justify anything from the rule of one man (Romulus was, after all, the first king) to the virtue of republics (his successors ended monarchy). Augustus built his principate on careful invocation of Romulus while carefully avoiding the word "king." Wikipedia: Romulus · Founding of Rome
